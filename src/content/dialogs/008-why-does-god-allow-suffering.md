### Why is there suffering?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

If God is loving and all-powerful, why is there so much suffering in the world? This is the question I keep coming back to — and I want to hear what the scriptures actually say, not just philosophical arguments.

</div>

#### Suffering aids spiritual growth

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh offers a profound perspective on suffering, suggesting that it plays a crucial role in the spiritual journey. He describes how "the true lover yearneth for tribulation" just as much as others yearn for forgiveness or mercy ([*Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This implies that suffering is not merely a burden but a means to deepen one's love and connection to the divine. Furthermore, Bahá'u'lláh states that "the sign of love is fortitude under My decree and patience under My trials," indicating that enduring hardships can be an expression of love and faith ([*Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). Through these trials, one's spiritual station is revealed, as "tribulations...sustained in Thy path" help to recognize true lovers of God ([*Prayers and Meditations*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8301)). This perspective transforms suffering from a mere affliction into a potential source of spiritual growth and divine connection.

</div>

### What about innocent suffering?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Okay, I get that suffering can be a part of spiritual growth, but what about innocent suffering, like kids getting sick or natural disasters? Is there anything in the Writings that directly addresses why those things happen, beyond just testing individual faith?

</div>

#### Part of a larger divine plan

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í Writings don't provide a straightforward explanation for why innocent suffering, like children's illnesses or natural disasters, occurs. However, Bahá'u'lláh touches on the "profound and often mysterious nature of existence," suggesting that these experiences might be part of a "larger divine plan" that goes beyond our understanding ([*Tablet of the Wondrous*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15019)). He refers to such events with terms like "wondrous sadness" and "wondrous death," hinting at their deeper spiritual significance, even if they are difficult to comprehend ([*Tablet of the Wondrous*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15019)). This perspective invites us to consider that there might be a hidden wisdom in these trials, even when they seem inexplicable.

</div>

Hold on, "wondrous sadness" and "wondrous death" sound pretty poetic, but where does Bahá'u'lláh actually link that to innocent suffering like natural disasters or children's illnesses? Are there specific writings that address that kind of suffering directly?

#### Suffering as providence and mercy

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh doesn't directly explain why innocent suffering like children's illnesses or natural disasters occur, but he offers a perspective on suffering as part of a larger spiritual journey. He describes calamity as "My providence," which outwardly appears as "fire and vengeance," but inwardly is "light and mercy" ([*Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This suggests that such trials, while painful, can lead to spiritual growth and understanding. He also emphasizes that "fortitude under My decree" and "patience under My trials" are signs of love ([*Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)), framing these experiences as opportunities for deeper connection with the divine.

</div>

### Purpose of suffering on a larger scale?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

Wait, but that still seems to focus more on individual spiritual growth rather than addressing the bigger picture — like why these events happen at all. #### Part of a divine plan

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh does indeed address the purpose of suffering on a larger scale, suggesting that what appears as "calamity" is actually "providence" in disguise ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15171)). He describes how, outwardly, these events might seem like "fire and vengeance," but inwardly they are "light and mercy" ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15171)). This implies that suffering isn't just about personal endurance but is part of a "larger divine plan" that leads to spiritual enlightenment and immortality ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15171)). So, while individual growth is a part of it, there's a broader purpose at play in the unfolding of these events.

</div>

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

#### Trials for spiritual growth

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh does indeed address the purpose of suffering, particularly in the *Kitáb-i-Íqán*. He suggests that such trials and tribulations occur so that "the souls of men may develop and be delivered from the prison-cage of self and desire" ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). This perspective frames suffering as a divine test, meant to "prove the peoples of the world" and distinguish "the pure and illuminated hearts" from the "perishable and barren soil" ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). It's a way to facilitate spiritual growth and reveal the true nature of individuals.

</div>
