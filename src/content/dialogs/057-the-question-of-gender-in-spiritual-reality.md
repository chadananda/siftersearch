If the soul has no gender (as some Bahá'í teachings suggest), why does earthly gender carry the weight it does in Bahá'í institutional structure? Is gender a feature of bodies only, or of souls too?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í teachings indeed emphasize that the soul has no gender. This understanding reflects the spiritual reality that souls are not defined by physical attributes, such as gender. However, the earthly experience, including gender, plays a significant role in the social and cultural dimensions of human life. 

In terms of Bahá'í institutional structures, gender distinctions have a historical and practical context. For example, the Universal House of Justice, the supreme governing body of the Bahá'í Faith, is currently composed only of men. This is based on instructions from Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá, and while the full wisdom behind this decision is not explicitly detailed, it is understood as part of the divine order revealed for this age.

Gender in this world is primarily a feature of bodies and the social constructs around them. The Bahá'í teachings also stress the importance of equality between men and women, aiming to transcend gender-based limitations as society progresses. The distinction in institutional roles does not imply inequality, but rather a different function within the framework of the Bahá'í administrative order, the deeper wisdom of which may unfold over time.

The exclusion of women from the Universal House of Justice is indeed a difficult issue to reconcile with the Bahá'í principle of gender equality. Here are some key points from the writings that address this topic:

1. **'Abdu'l-Bahá's Explanation**: 'Abdu'l-Bahá, in response to questions regarding women's exclusion from the Universal House of Justice, explained that this is based on a specific provision of Bahá'í law. He indicated that the wisdom behind this decision would become clear in the future, comparing it to the clarity of the sun. 

   > "’Abdu’l-Bahá states in a Tablet that it is confined to men, and that the wisdom of it will be revealed as manifest as the sun in the future." ([Messages 1963 to 1986](https://siftersearch.com/document/26/1351) — Universal House of Justice)

2. **Different Functions, Not Inequality**: The writings emphasize that the exclusion of women from this specific institution should not imply inherent inequality between the sexes. It is a matter of differing functions within the Bahá'í administrative structure.

   > "As ’Abdu’l-Bahá Himself has explicitly stated that sexes are equal except in some cases, the exclusion of women from the International House of Justice should not be surprising." ([Messages 1963 to 1986](https://siftersearch.com/document/26/1351) — Universal House of Justice)

3. **Gender Equality as a Core Principle**: Despite this specific exclusion, the broader Bahá'í teachings strongly emphasize gender equality as a fundamental principle. Bahá'í writings call for full gender equality in all areas of life, indicating this as an essential aspect of social progress.

   > "The Bahá’í Writings also call unreservedly for recognition of the equality of women and men, and for full 'gender equality' in all areas." ([Questions from Christians](https://siftersearch.com/document/7149/831) — Thom Thompson)

The rationale for this specific practice is not fully explained in the available texts, which can certainly be challenging for those who are deeply committed to the principle of equality. Bahá'ís are encouraged to approach these issues with faith in the progressive unfolding of divine wisdom. Critical engagement and open dialogue are important, and Bahá'ís are invited to explore these questions thoughtfully, maintaining a balance between reasoned inquiry and spiritual trust.
