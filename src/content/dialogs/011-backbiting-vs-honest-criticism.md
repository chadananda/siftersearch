### Is criticism of power backbiting

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

The Bahá'í writings strongly forbid backbiting. But honest criticism of public figures, of the powerful, of injustice — is that backbiting? Where exactly is the line?

</div>

#### Criticism differs from backbiting

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í writings draw a clear line between backbiting and constructive criticism. Backbiting is described as ["the greatest sin of humanity"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/bahaullah-abdul-baha_tablets-of-bahaullah-and-abdul-baha-from-star-of-the-west#p695), a "fiendish instrument" that sows discord and harms unity. However, Shoghi Effendi acknowledges that ["vicious criticism is indeed a calamity"](https://oceanlibrary.com/arohanui_shoghi-effendi/?paraId=para_220), suggesting that criticism itself isn't inherently wrong but becomes problematic when it lacks faith and obedience to Bahá'u'lláh's teachings. The key is intention and approach: criticism should aim to uplift and improve rather than to belittle or undermine.

</div>

### Other scriptures on criticism

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Hold on — what do the Gospels or the Qur'án say about criticism and backbiting? You're only showing me the Bahá'í angle.

</div>

#### Gospels and Qur'án on criticism

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Gospels and the Qur'án both address the issue of backbiting and criticism, albeit in different contexts. In the *Gospel of Luke*, Jesus himself faced mockery and mistreatment, as it is noted that ["the men that held Jesus mocked him"](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-luke/?paraId=para_815). This highlights the human tendency to criticize unjustly, especially those in positions of vulnerability. Meanwhile, the Qur'án advises against backbiting, likening it to eating the flesh of one's dead brother, which is a powerful metaphor for the destructive nature of such behavior. Both texts suggest that while criticism can be necessary, it should be approached with caution and integrity, avoiding personal attacks and slander.

</div>

### Constructive criticism in scriptures

<div class="user-turn" id="round-3">

Wait, that's interesting about the Qur'án's metaphor. But what about constructive criticism? How do these texts distinguish between harmful backbiting and criticism that aims to address injustice or hold power to account?

</div>

#### Gentle approach advised

<div class="jafar-turn">

When it comes to distinguishing between harmful backbiting and constructive criticism, the Qur'án advises a gentle approach. It suggests that when addressing those in power, one should ["speak to him soft words, haply he may understand and believe"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/abdul-baha_the-secret-of-divine-civilization#p220), as seen in the advice given to Moses and Aaron. Bahá'u'lláh also emphasizes that words should be used wisely, noting that while they can be a source of ["Divine mercy"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/bahaullah_tablet-of-unity-lawh-i-ittihad-2#p54), if they exceed their bounds, they become destructive. This suggests that criticism should aim to educate and uplift rather than harm.

</div>

### Specific teachings on constructive criticism

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

Hold on, that Bahá'u'lláh quote is useful, but it sounds more like a general principle about words. Are there specific teachings in the Qur'án or the Gospels about how to criticize constructively? Not just avoid harming, but really address injustice or hold power to account effectively?

</div>

#### Private correction emphasized

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, both the Qur'án and the Gospels offer guidance on how to address injustice constructively. The Qur'án advises that when speaking truth to power, one should ["speak to him soft words"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/abdul-baha_the-secret-of-divine-civilization#p220), suggesting a gentle approach to encourage understanding and belief. In the Gospels, Jesus emphasizes the importance of addressing wrongs with a spirit of reconciliation, as he instructs to first ["go and tell him his fault between you and him alone"](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-matthew/?paraId=para_323), *Gospel of Matthew*. This approach aims to resolve issues directly and privately, fostering a spirit of constructive dialogue rather than public confrontation.

</div>

### Public criticism of leaders

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

Wait, those are helpful examples. But are there any texts that deal with public criticism of leaders? It seems like private or gentle correction doesn't always apply in addressing systemic injustice. How do these traditions suggest holding power accountable in a public way?

</div>

#### Nuanced guidance for public critique

<div class="jafar-turn">

When it comes to public criticism of leaders, the traditions offer nuanced guidance. Bahá'u'lláh addresses rulers directly, urging them to ["lend your ears unto that whereof the Supreme admonisheth you"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/bahaullah_tablet-to-queen-victoria-lawh-i-malikih-2#p35), which implies a responsibility to be open to critique. In the Qur'án, the approach to speaking truth to power is exemplified by the command to Moses and Aaron to ["speak to him soft words"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/abdul-baha_the-secret-of-divine-civilization#p220) when addressing Pharaoh, suggesting that even when confronting injustice, the manner of speech matters. Shoghi Effendi warns against "vicious criticism" and emphasizes that it stems from a lack of faith and obedience, suggesting that constructive engagement should align with spiritual principles and community processes. These teachings collectively underscore the importance of addressing injustice with both courage and wisdom.

</div>
