The English word 'spiritual' has drifted enormously over the last century. When 'Abdu'l-Bahá used the word, did it carry our modern New-Age connotations, or something more austere?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

When 'Abdu'l-Bahá spoke of "spiritual" matters, he was referring to something much deeper and more profound than the modern New-Age connotations we often associate with the term today. His use of "spiritual" was rooted in the transformational journey of the soul, the cultivation of virtues, and the alignment of one's life with divine principles.

In the context of 'Abdu'l-Bahá's writings, spirituality is about the development of moral and ethical qualities, the deepening of one's understanding of God's will, and the commitment to serving humanity. It's less about personal enlightenment or mystical experiences, and more about living a life of service, unity, and love. This understanding is consistent with the broader Bahá'í teachings, which emphasize the importance of character and action over abstract spiritual experiences.

> "Truly mystical experiences based on reality are very rare, and we can readily see how dangerous it is for people to go groping about in the darkness of their imagination after the true thing. This perspective fits within the broader Bahá'í framework that prioritizes ethical and moral development over purely personal mystical experiences. The focus is on the fruits of spirituality—how these experiences translate into actions that benefit others and contribute to one's growth in virtues. This approach suggests that while mystical experiences can be part of one's spiritual journey, they are not the ultimate goal and should not overshadow the core tasks of moral and ethical development.
