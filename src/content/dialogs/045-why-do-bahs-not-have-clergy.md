<div class="user-turn">

The Bahá'í Faith deliberately abolished clergy. What's the rationale, and what fills the function — pastoral care, theology, ritual leadership — that clergy provide in other traditions?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The abolition of clergy in the Bahá'í Faith is rooted in the principle of individual investigation of truth and the belief in a direct relationship between the individual and God. Bahá'u'lláh emphasized the importance of each person seeking truth independently, free from the intermediary role that clergy traditionally play in other religions.

In place of clergy, the Bahá'í community relies on a unique administrative structure comprising elected bodies at the local, national, and international levels. Local Spiritual Assemblies provide community guidance and support, addressing pastoral needs and organizing community events, while the Universal House of Justice offers global leadership and guidance on theological and administrative matters.

For personal spiritual growth and understanding, Bahá'ís are encouraged to study the teachings of Bahá'u'lláh, 'Abdu'l-Bahá, and other authoritative texts. Community gatherings, such as devotional meetings and study circles, also play a role in fostering spiritual development and education.

Ritual leadership is minimized, as Bahá'í practices are simple and designed to be accessible to all believers, further reinforcing the idea that each individual is responsible for their spiritual journey.

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í Faith addresses the potential for misinterpretation and fragmentation through its unique administrative framework and the authority of its institutions. Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá emphasized the unity and cohesion of the community through clear texts and the establishment of institutions rather than a clergy.

The Universal House of Justice plays a crucial role in maintaining theological unity. While it does not interpret scripture authoritatively, it has the authority to legislate on matters not explicitly covered in the sacred texts, ensuring the community adapts to contemporary needs while staying true to Bahá'í principles. Shoghi Effendi's interpretations also provide a strong foundation for understanding Bahá'í teachings.

> "Unity of doctrine is maintained by the existence of the authentic texts of Scripture and the voluminous interpretations of ’Abdu’l-Bahá and Shoghi Effendi, together with the absolute prohibition against anyone propounding 'authoritative' or 'inspired' interpretations or usurping the function of Guardian." ([*Messages from the Universal House of Justice: 1986-2001*](https://siftersearch.com/documents/366) — Universal House of Justice)

The administrative structure, including Local Spiritual Assemblies and the National Spiritual Assemblies, ensures that the community remains unified and can address pastoral and theological needs without the need for clergy. This system helps prevent fragmentation by relying on collective decision-making and consultation, guided by the principles laid out in the Bahá'í writings.

While individuals are free to explore and interpret the Bahá'í writings, they are also encouraged to do so within the framework provided by authoritative interpretations and the guidance of the administrative institutions. This approach helps prevent rigid dogmatism while maintaining coherence.

Shoghi Effendi, as the appointed Guardian of the Bahá'í Faith, provided authoritative interpretations that have clarified complex theological and administrative issues, helping to ensure a unified understanding. One example is his work *"The Dispensation of Bahá'u'lláh,"* where he explains the station of Bahá'u'lláh, 'Abdu'l-Bahá, and himself, thus solidifying the understanding of leadership and authority in the Faith.

When differences in understanding arise at the grassroots level, Bahá'ís are encouraged to engage in consultation—a distinctive method of decision-making and problem-solving that emphasizes open dialogue, mutual respect, and a collective search for truth. This process allows for diverse views to be expressed and considered, fostering a spirit of unity even in the presence of differing perspectives.

Local Spiritual Assemblies play an essential role in guiding community members and ensuring that discussions and interpretations remain aligned with Bahá'í teachings. They provide support and resources to help deepen individuals' understanding of the writings, promoting a cohesive community life while respecting personal exploration.
