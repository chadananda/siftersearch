How do Bahá'ís understand Muhammad's role given that the Bahá'í writings are sometimes critical of how Muslim communities have interpreted Islam? Where is the line?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í Faith holds a deep respect for Muhammad, recognizing Him as a Manifestation of God who delivered a message of unity and spiritual empowerment suited for His time. Bahá'ís see Muhammad as a vital link in the chain of divine guidance, which includes figures like Moses, Jesus, the Buddha, and others, leading up to Bahá'u'lláh in a progressive revelation.

The Bahá'í writings sometimes critique the interpretations and actions of religious communities, including Muslims, when these interpretations lead to division, intolerance, or practices that stray from the original spirit of the Prophet's teachings. This critique is not unique to Islam; Bahá'í texts also address similar issues in other religious communities. The line generally drawn is between the divine message itself, which is honored and revered, and the human interpretations or institutional practices that may have deviated from that message over time.

Bahá'u'lláh, in His writings, emphasizes the unity of all religions and the need to transcend outdated interpretations that cause division. The aim is to return to the essence of the prophetic teachings which advocate for justice, compassion, and the oneness of humanity.

> "In such passages as these Jesus makes manifest how spiritual evolution is for ever continuous through unceasing change, and how one Prophet of God succeeding another shows forth by word and by example a Truth which in its aspect and in the degree of its revelation is always altering, but in its source and in its essence is for ever the same." ([*The Heart of the Gospel*](https://siftersearch.com/doc/7123) — George Townshend)

This illustrates the Bahá'í belief in the unity of all religions, each contributing to humanity's spiritual and social progress, while recognizing the need for renewal and reinterpretation to meet the evolving needs of society.
