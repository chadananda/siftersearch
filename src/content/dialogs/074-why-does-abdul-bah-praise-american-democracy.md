## You

'Abdu'l-Bahá's Tablets of the Divine Plan praise American democracy and assign America a unique role. Was this prophecy, contextual diplomacy, or something else?

## Jafar

'Abdu'l-Bahá's praise of American democracy and the unique role he assigns to the United States in the Tablets of the Divine Plan can be seen from multiple perspectives. It can indeed be understood as a form of prophecy, given the significant role the United States has played in the global spread of the Bahá'í Faith and its principles. However, it's also possible to see it as an acknowledgment of the potential that democratic principles hold for advancing unity and justice — core tenets of the Bahá'í Faith.

In another light, his words might reflect a diplomatic approach, encouraging the American believers by recognizing their country's strengths and potential for leadership in fostering international harmony. 'Abdu'l-Bahá’s insights often encompass a combination of spiritual foresight and practical encouragement, aimed at motivating individuals and communities to rise to their spiritual and social responsibilities.

## You

Also, if we consider the diplomatic angle, how does this praise compare to other instances where 'Abdu'l-Bahá spoke about political systems or governance in different countries? Did he employ similar language or strategies elsewhere, and how does that inform our understanding of his message to America?

## Jafar

When 'Abdu'l-Bahá speaks about America's role, it's more about recognizing latent potential rather than a specific foretelling of future events. His vision aligns with Bahá'í ideals of unity, justice, and the capacity for moral leadership. The United States, with its democratic principles and diverse population, represented an opportunity for these ideals to manifest on a broad scale. 

Regarding the diplomatic angle, 'Abdu'l-Bahá often tailored his messages to the unique circumstances and strengths of different countries. For example, when he visited Europe and spoke about peace and reconciliation, he highlighted values like cooperation and understanding across borders — principles that resonated deeply in a post-war context. Similarly, in the Middle East, he emphasized the spiritual heritage of the region, urging unity among diverse religious and cultural groups.

This approach demonstrates his strategic encouragement, focusing on each region's strengths and potential contributions to a unified world. In America, the emphasis on democracy and freedom served as a call to action for the Bahá'ís there, urging them to lead by example in realizing the principles of the Bahá'í Faith.

## You

## Jafar

'Abdu'l-Bahá did indeed acknowledge the societal flaws present in America during his time, particularly around issues of racial division. He spoke candidly about the need for racial unity and harmony, emphasizing the importance of bridging the racial divide as a critical step for America to fulfill its potential.

In his talks, he often highlighted the moral courage displayed during the Civil War, noting the sacrifices made for the freedom of enslaved peoples. He recognized this as a significant step towards justice and equality, yet he also pointed out that true unity required more than political solutions — it demanded a transformation of hearts and a commitment to genuine equality and interracial fellowship.

For instance, 'Abdu'l-Bahá encouraged gatherings that included both black and white individuals, advocating for love and unity that transcended racial boundaries. He even suggested interracial marriage as a means to heal racial divisions, which was a radical proposal at the time.

> "If it be possible, gather together these two races, black and white, into one assembly, and put such love into their hearts that they shall not only unite but even intermarry." ([239 Days in America: Compilation of Essays from 239days.com](https://siftersearch.com))

These statements show that while 'Abdu'l-Bahá recognized America's potential, he also called out its current shortcomings and laid out a vision for overcoming them through love, unity, and justice. Thus, his praise of America was not blind to its flaws but rather an invitation to rise above them.
