## You

'Abdu'l-Bahá speaks of a 'mystic sense' that perceives spiritual realities the analytic intellect cannot. What is this sense, and how does one know it from imagination or wishful thinking?

## Jafar

The "mystic sense" 'Abdu'l-Bahá refers to is an intuitive faculty that allows individuals to perceive spiritual truths beyond the reach of the analytical mind. This sense is akin to a spiritual intuition or insight that transcends ordinary perception and logical reasoning. It's the capacity to connect with deeper realities and truths that are often veiled from the material senses.

Distinguishing this mystic sense from imagination or wishful thinking involves cultivating discernment and spiritual discipline. One way is through consistent practice of prayer, meditation, and reflection, which can help refine one's spiritual perception. Additionally, aligning one's life with ethical principles and virtues can purify the heart and make it more receptive to genuine spiritual insights. The key is to develop a grounded spiritual practice that fosters both humility and openness, allowing for the genuine mystic sense to be distinguished from mere fancy. 

This is not an easy task, and it often requires guidance from those more experienced in spiritual matters, as well as a sincere intention to seek truth for its own sake.

## You

You mentioned that cultivating this mystic sense involves discernment and spiritual discipline, but how can one be certain that even with prayer, meditation, and reflection, what one perceives isn't just a product of deep-seated desires or subconscious biases? Is there a criterion or a litmus test given in the Bahá'í writings that helps verify the authenticity of these insights? And doesn't the need for guidance from those more experienced suggest a reliance on external validation rather than an internal realization?

## Jafar

These are profound questions, and they touch on the heart of spiritual discernment. In the Bahá'í writings, there's an understanding that spiritual insights must align with certain criteria to be considered authentic. One key criterion is consistency with the teachings of the Manifestations of God, which act as a standard for measuring spiritual truths. If an insight or intuition contradicts these teachings, it's likely to be a product of one's imagination or bias.

Furthermore, 'Abdu'l-Bahá emphasizes the importance of detachment and humility. A genuine mystic sense should lead to greater love, compassion, and service, rather than self-aggrandizement or conflict. The fruits of the insight — whether they lead to constructive and harmonious action — can also be a measure of its authenticity.

Regarding guidance from others, it's not about external validation in a superficial sense. Rather, it's about recognizing that spiritual understanding often benefits from the insights and experiences of those who have walked the path longer. This doesn't replace internal realization but can complement and confirm it. It's akin to consulting a map when navigating unfamiliar territory; it doesn't replace the journey but can help ensure one is on the right path. Ultimately, the Bahá'í faith encourages a balance between personal insight and the wisdom found in community and teachings.
