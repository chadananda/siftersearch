<div class="jafar-turn">

The Conference of Badasht was a pivotal event in the early Bahá'í history, taking place in 1848 in Persia (modern-day Iran). It brought together key followers of the Báb, and it was during this gathering that significant discussions about the nature and direction of the new movement occurred.

Tahirih, a remarkable and influential figure in the Bábí and Bahá'í Faiths, made a dramatic gesture by removing her veil in front of the assembled participants. This act was deeply symbolic and controversial, as it defied the strict social norms and religious laws of the time, which mandated women to wear veils in public.

The removal of her veil was not merely an act of defiance but a profound declaration of the dawn of a new era — the abrogation of past religious laws and the ushering in of a new dispensation. It signified the break from the old order and the embrace of a new spiritual and social reality. For many, it was a call to recognize the fundamental changes that the Bábí movement was bringing about, including the advancement of women's roles and rights.

This moment is considered foundational in the Bahá'í tradition because it exemplified the courage and vision of the early believers and symbolized the transformative changes that the new faith sought to achieve. Tahirih's bold action remains a powerful emblem of the progress towards gender equality and the liberation from outdated customs, themes that are central to the Bahá'í teachings.
