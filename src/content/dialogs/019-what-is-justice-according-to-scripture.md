### Meaning of justice in scriptures

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

The word 'justice' is everywhere in religious texts, but politicians and activists also use it constantly with very different meanings. What do the primary scriptures actually mean by justice — and how does that compare to modern usage?

</div>

#### Justice as spiritual and moral integrity

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Bahá'í writings, justice is deeply intertwined with spiritual and moral integrity. Bahá'u'lláh describes justice as "the best beloved of all things in My sight," urging individuals to "see with thine own eyes and not through the eyes of others" ([*Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This suggests a personal responsibility to seek truth and fairness, rather than relying on societal norms or external opinions. In contrast, modern political and activist uses of justice often focus on systemic changes and social equity, which, while important, may not always emphasize this inner moral dimension. Bahá'u'lláh also warns against hypocrisy, stating that one who "biddeth men be just and himself committeth iniquity is not of Me" ([*Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This highlights the importance of aligning one's actions with one's advocacy, a principle that can sometimes be overlooked in contemporary discourse.

</div>

### Connection of justice to personal insight

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Wait, can we dig into that quote from the Hidden Words a bit more? How does "seeing with your own eyes" connect to justice? It sounds like it's more about personal insight or truth-seeking than the broader social changes that politicians talk about.

</div>

#### Justice begins with personal discernment

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh's phrase "see with thine own eyes" indeed highlights the significance of personal insight in the pursuit of justice ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/20809)). He encourages individuals to "know of thine own knowledge and not through the knowledge of thy neighbor," suggesting that justice begins with personal discernment and understanding ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/20809)). This approach contrasts with the broader social changes often discussed in political contexts, emphasizing that true justice is "My gift to thee and the sign of My loving-kindness," rooted in individual moral clarity ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/20809)).

</div>

### Personal and social dimensions of justice

<div class="user-turn" id="round-3">

Hold on — that does make it sound like Bahá'u'lláh is framing justice as something deeply personal and spiritual. But isn't there also a social dimension to justice in the Bahá'í teachings? Like, how does personal insight translate into collective justice? How do you square that with the ideas about justice in *The Promised Day Is Come* or the world order letters?

</div>

#### Justice has personal and social dimensions

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, Bahá'u'lláh's teachings on justice do have both a personal and a social dimension. On a personal level, justice is about "seeing with thine own eyes" and knowing through one's own understanding, which encourages individuals to seek truth independently ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/20809)). This personal insight is foundational, but it also extends into the social realm. Shoghi Effendi highlights that leaders are to "rule with justice," safeguarding the rights of the downtrodden and ensuring equity in governance ([*The Promised Day is Come*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8302)). So, personal integrity in justice becomes the bedrock for collective justice, where just leaders act as "the shadow of God on earth," reflecting divine justice in their actions ([*The Promised Day is Come*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8302)).

</div>

### Source of 'shadow of God' phrase

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

Okay, but the "shadow of God on earth" phrase you're quoting is from Shoghi Effendi, right? We need to be careful because that's more of a commentary. Can we back up your point with something direct from Bahá'u'lláh or 'Abdu'l-Bahá? How do they describe the connection between personal and social justice?

</div>

#### Personal integrity as foundation for justice

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, Bahá'u'lláh himself ties personal and social justice together quite directly. He describes justice as "the best beloved of all things in My sight" and emphasizes that it enables one to "see with thine own eyes" rather than relying on others' perceptions ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This personal discernment is crucial for recognizing and upholding justice in society. Moreover, he warns that someone who "biddeth men be just and himself committeth iniquity" is not truly aligned with His teachings, highlighting the importance of personal integrity as a foundation for social justice ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)).

</div>

### Justice in community life and governance

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

That makes sense — personal integrity sets the stage for social justice. But what about justice as a principle for community life or governance? Does Bahá'u'lláh offer guidance on how justice should shape institutions or how leaders should approach justice? I feel like we're missing how the personal ties into broader systems.

</div>

#### Justice as principle for governance

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh indeed offers guidance on how justice should shape both personal and community life. He emphasizes that justice is "the best beloved of all things in My sight," urging individuals and leaders to "turn not away therefrom" if they desire His trust ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This highlights that justice is not just a personal virtue but a foundational principle for governance. He also warns "oppressors on Earth" to "withdraw your hands from tyranny," making it clear that justice must be embodied by those in positions of authority ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This connection between personal integrity and social justice is essential for creating just institutions.

</div>
