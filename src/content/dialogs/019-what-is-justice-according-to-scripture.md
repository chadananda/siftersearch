### Meaning of justice in scriptures

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

The word 'justice' is everywhere in religious texts, but politicians and activists also use it constantly with very different meanings. What do the primary scriptures actually mean by justice — and how does that compare to modern usage?

</div>

#### Justice as personal and divine principle

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh frames justice as a deeply personal and divine principle, calling it "the best beloved of all things" and a "gift" that allows individuals to "see with thine own eyes" and "know of thine own knowledge" ([*The Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This suggests that justice is not merely about external laws or societal norms but is an inner quality that guides one's perception and understanding. In contrast, modern usage often focuses on justice as a social or political construct, emphasizing fairness and equality in societal systems. Bahá'u'lláh also emphasizes justice as a "lamp that showeth to human beings a path in the darkness," highlighting its role in guiding moral and ethical behavior ([*Tablet to Mírzá Abu'l-Faḍl*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15762)). This spiritual dimension may be less emphasized in contemporary discussions, which often prioritize legal and institutional frameworks.

</div>

### Justice in community and society

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Okay, but what about the idea of justice in terms of community and society? In the *Kitáb-i-Aqdas*, Bahá'u'lláh talks about laws and governance. How does that fit with what you're saying about justice as a personal quality?

</div>

#### Justice as personal and societal cornerstone

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh indeed presents justice as both a personal virtue and a cornerstone for societal governance. In the *Kitáb-i-Aqdas*, He calls for the establishment of "Houses of Justice" in every city, where members are to "consider themselves as entering the Court of the presence of God" ([*Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). These institutions are meant to act as "trustees and representatives of God," ensuring that justice is upheld within the community ([*Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). This dual role of justice underscores its significance in shaping both personal conduct and the collective well-being of society.

</div>

### Role of Houses of Justice

<div class="user-turn" id="round-3">

Wait, so are the Houses of Justice supposed to enforce laws like modern governments do, or is their role different? How does that connect to justice as a personal virtue?

</div>

#### Houses as divine trustees, not governments

<div class="jafar-turn">

The role of the Houses of Justice is indeed different from modern governments. Bahá'u'lláh envisions them as "trustees and representatives of God" who must act with a sense of divine responsibility ([*Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). They are to "observe pure justice" and ensure the welfare of the community, which ties their governance to the spiritual and moral dimensions of justice ([*Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). This means that while they do enforce laws, their actions are meant to reflect the personal virtue of justice, emphasizing integrity and care for the public good.

</div>

### Conflict between personal and community justice

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

But what happens if the community's idea of justice conflicts with an individual's personal sense of justice? How do you reconcile those two, especially when the House of Justice is expected to act with divine responsibility?

</div>

#### Adherence to divine principles for unity

<div class="jafar-turn">

When an individual's personal sense of justice conflicts with the community's, Bahá'u'lláh encourages adherence to the principles set by the Houses of Justice, which are seen as "representatives of divine authority" ([*Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). He warns against letting "the desires of the flesh and of a corrupt inclination provoke divisions," emphasizing the need for unity and harmony ([*Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). This approach suggests that while personal justice is important, it should align with the broader framework of divine laws to maintain societal cohesion.

</div>

### Individual freedom vs. community justice

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

Okay, but doesn't that raise questions about individual freedom and conscience? If someone feels a deep sense of injustice personally, are they just supposed to set it aside in favor of unity? How does that fit with the idea of "seeing with thine own eyes"?

</div>

#### Freedom in submission to divine guidance

<div class="jafar-turn">

It's a thoughtful question about balancing personal conscience with community unity. Bahá'u'lláh suggests that "true liberty consisteth in man's submission unto My commandments," indicating that freedom is found in aligning with divine guidance rather than purely personal judgment ([*The Most Holy Book (Kitáb-i-Aqdas)*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). He acknowledges that "liberty must, in the end, lead to sedition" if not tempered by such guidance, implying that unchecked personal freedom can lead to chaos ([*The Most Holy Book (Kitáb-i-Aqdas)*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)). So, while personal feelings of injustice are important, the teachings encourage individuals to "put himself under laws" that protect against ignorance and harm, fostering a collective sense of justice ([*The Most Holy Book (Kitáb-i-Aqdas)*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712)).

</div>
