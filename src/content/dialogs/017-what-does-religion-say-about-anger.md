### Is anger always a sin?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

I struggle with anger. Is anger always a sin in religious teachings, or is there such a thing as righteous anger? What do the scriptures — across traditions — actually teach about it?

</div>

#### Anger isn't inherently sinful

<div class="jafar-turn">

It's interesting to see how various traditions approach anger. In Bahá'í thought, anger isn't inherently sinful, but it must be "tempered" and "disciplined" to avoid becoming destructive ([*Bahá’í Ethics in Light of Scripture, Vol. 2*](https://siftersearch.com/document/523)). 'Abdu'l-Bahá suggests that anger can be "praiseworthy" when directed against injustice, like "bloodthirsty tyrants" ([*Bahá’í Ethics in Light of Scripture, Vol. 2*](https://siftersearch.com/document/523)). This aligns with broader ethical teachings, such as Aristotle's idea that being "angry at the right things" and in the right way is actually commendable ([*Bahá’í Ethics in Light of Scripture, Vol. 2*](https://siftersearch.com/document/523)). So, while anger can be a force for good, it requires careful management to ensure it serves a moral purpose.

</div>

#### Self-control emphasized in Hidden Words

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh touches on the theme of anger in "The Hidden Words," emphasizing the need for self-control. He warns that unchecked passion can lead one "into perilous ways that lead unto destruction" ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This suggests that anger, if not managed, can be destructive. Additionally, he advises that when "the fire of self" overcomes you, it's important to "remember your own faults" rather than focusing on others' ([*The Hidden Words of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This reflection can help temper anger and guide one's spiritual journey.

</div>

#### Self-reflection ties to humility

<div class="jafar-turn">

You're right in seeing that Bahá'u'lláh doesn't condemn anger itself, but rather the "lack of self-control" over it. He suggests that reflecting on our own faults can be a powerful tool for managing anger. In "The Hidden Words," Bahá'u'lláh advises us to "forget thine own faults and busy thyself with the faults of others," highlighting how self-reflection can prevent us from being overly critical of others ([*The Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This practice ties into the broader theme of humility, as it encourages us to "remember your own faults" and recognize our imperfections, fostering a more compassionate and understanding approach to others ([*The Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)).

</div>

Hold on — you flipped that quote. It's "forget the faults of others and busy thyself with thine own." Bahá'u'lláh is guiding us to focus inwardly on self-improvement instead of criticizing others. So, how does that shift in focus help us manage anger more effectively?

#### Focus inward for anger management

<div class="jafar-turn">

Focusing inwardly on self-improvement, as Bahá'u'lláh guides us, can indeed help manage anger more effectively. By remembering "your own faults and not the faults of My creatures," we cultivate self-awareness and humility, which are powerful tools against anger ([*The Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). This inward focus fosters empathy and understanding, allowing us to "breathe not the sins of others" and instead reflect on our own behavior ([*The Hidden Words*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8230)). Such a shift in focus aligns with the broader Bahá'í theme of humility, encouraging personal improvement over judgment of others.

</div>
