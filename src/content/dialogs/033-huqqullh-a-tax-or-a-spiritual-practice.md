Is the Right of God (Huqúqu'lláh) properly understood as a kind of religious tax, or is it more like a spiritual practice? What does the textual rationale say?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

Huqúqu'lláh, or the Right of God, is a unique institution within the Bahá'í Faith. While it might appear similar to a religious tax, its deeper nature and purpose align more closely with a spiritual practice. Bahá'u'lláh describes Huqúqu'lláh as a "great law" and emphasizes its spiritual significance as "the source of grace, abundance, and of all good," which remains with the soul across all worlds of God:

> "Ḥuqúqu’lláh is indeed a great law. It is incumbent upon all to make this offering, because it is the source of grace, abundance, and of all good. It is a bounty which shall remain with every soul in every world of the worlds of God, the All-Possessing, the All-Bountiful." ([*Lights of Guidance*](https://siftersearch.com/passage/8680/2171) — Bahá’u’lláh)

This passage indicates that Huqúqu'lláh is not merely a financial obligation but an act of devotion that brings spiritual benefits to the giver. It reflects a believer's love and commitment to God, contributing to the community's welfare and the Faith's growth. The offering is made voluntarily and is calculated based on surplus wealth, emphasizing its spiritual dimension rather than being a compulsory tax.
