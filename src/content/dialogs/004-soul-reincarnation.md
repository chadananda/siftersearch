## You

How does the Bahá'í concept of the soul differ from the Hindu and Buddhist concepts of reincarnation? I want a sharp answer — not "all religions teach about the soul" but the actual ontological differences.

## Jafar

The Bahá'í concept of the soul, unlike Hindu and Buddhist reincarnation, emphasizes a singular, eternal progression after physical death rather than repeated earthly lives. The lack of specific Bahá'í passages found here may reflect this distinct perspective, focusing on eternal development beyond the physical.

In Hinduism, reincarnation is a cycle of birth, death, and rebirth, driven by karma:
> "Reincarnation is not easily understood by a thoughtless child deluded by the delusion of wealth, name or fame. Everything ends with death, he thinks, and thus falls again and again under the sway of death." (*Five Lectures on Reincarnation* — Swami Abhedananda)

In Buddhism, reincarnation is part of the broader cycle of samsara, with liberation as the ultimate goal:
> "Within the realm of Buddhist philosophy, the doctrine of reincarnation is conspicuous by reason of its peculiarly attractive charms." (*The Diamond Sutra* — tr. William Gemmell)

These concepts emphasize cycles and liberation, contrasting with the Bahá'í view of a linear spiritual journey.
