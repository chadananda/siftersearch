### Distinction in Bahá'í consultation practice

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

Bahá'í consultation is often described as a spiritual practice, not just a decision-making method. What does that distinction mean in practice?

</div>

#### Consultation as spiritual practice

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'í consultation is indeed more than just a method for making decisions; it is a spiritual practice that requires participants to embody certain virtues. Shoghi Effendi outlines the "prime requisites" for consultation, which include "purity of motive, radiance of spirit, detachment from all else save God, attraction to His Divine Fragrances, humility and lowliness" among others [*Bahá’í Administration*](https://oceanlibrary.com/bahai-administration_shoghi-effendi/?paraId=para_33). These qualities transform the process into a collective spiritual exercise, where the goal is not merely to reach a decision, but to achieve a unity of thought and purpose that reflects divine guidance. This approach contrasts with typical decision-making processes, as it emphasizes spiritual growth and unity over individual opinions or outcomes.

</div>

#### Gospels and Qur'án on decision-making

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, the idea of decision-making as a spiritual practice is present in both the Gospels and the Qur'án. In the *Gospel of Matthew*, Jesus emphasizes the importance of discernment, urging those who ["have ears to hear, let them hear"](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-matthew/?paraId=para_323), suggesting a deeper listening that goes beyond mere words. The Qur'án similarly encourages believers to seek divine guidance, emphasizing that ["we trust in God in all matters"](https://oceanlibrary.com/abdul-bahas-commentary-stages-of-soul_moojan-momen/?paraId=para_117), which frames decision-making as a reliance on divine wisdom. Both traditions highlight the inner, spiritual dimension of making choices, aligning closely with the Bahá'í view of consultation as a spiritual practice.

</div>

#### Parables and Istikhara as examples

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Gospels, decision-making is often portrayed as a spiritual act through parables and teachings. For instance, Jesus emphasizes the need for discernment by saying, ["Who hath ears to hear, let him hear"](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-matthew/?paraId=para_323), *Gospel of Matthew*. This suggests that understanding and making decisions require spiritual insight. In the Qur'án, decision-making is also intertwined with divine guidance. The text often emphasizes seeking God's counsel, as seen in the practice of Istikhara, where believers pray for guidance in making choices. Both traditions highlight the importance of aligning one's decisions with spiritual principles and divine will.

#### Prodigal Son and Joseph stories

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Gospels, the story of the [Prodigal Son](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-matthew/?paraId=para_323) is a powerful example of decision-making as a spiritual act. The son's journey from squandering his inheritance to returning home in humility reflects a deep inner transformation and a conscious choice to seek forgiveness. Similarly, in the Qur'án, the story of [Joseph](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/abdul-baha_the-secret-of-divine-civilization-2#p154) illustrates decision-making as a spiritual process. Joseph's steadfastness and reliance on God during his trials demonstrate how spiritual discernment guides actions. Both narratives highlight the profound spiritual dimensions involved in making choices.
