## You

The Báb's Bayán contains laws and teachings that Bahá'u'lláh subsequently abrogated or fulfilled. What was the Bayán actually teaching, and how should a Bahá'í today read it?

## Jafar

The Bayán, revealed by the Báb, served as a bridge between the Islamic dispensation and the Bahá'í Faith. It introduced new laws and concepts aimed at preparing the way for "He Whom God shall make manifest," a figure whom Bahá'ís recognize as Bahá'u'lláh. The Báb's teachings emphasized spiritual renewal, the oneness of humanity, and the coming of a greater revelation.

For Bahá'ís today, the Bayán is read with an understanding that many of its specific laws were provisional, meant for a transitional period. Bahá'u'lláh, in fulfilling the Báb's prophecy, brought a new set of laws and teachings for this age, which abrogated certain aspects of the Bayán. However, the Bayán remains a profound spiritual document, offering insight into the nature of progressive revelation and the divine plan.

Bahá'ís approach the Bayán with reverence, drawing inspiration from its spiritual vision while adhering to the laws and teachings of Bahá'u'lláh. Its purpose was not only to reform the religious practices of its time but also to herald the unity and harmony that Bahá'u'lláh's revelation would bring.

## Jafar

One specific teaching of the Bayán that illustrates the themes of spiritual renewal and oneness is its emphasis on the recognition of a new Manifestation of God, "He Whom God shall make manifest." This concept was revolutionary, encouraging followers to look beyond traditional religious boundaries and prepare for a new era of unity and spiritual insight. The Bayán called for a transformation of the heart and mind, urging people to detach from outdated customs and embrace a future of unity.

Regarding the abrogation of laws and the concept of progressive revelation, it's important to understand that each Manifestation of God brings teachings tailored to the needs and capacities of the time. Abrogation is not viewed as a contradiction but as an evolution. Just as a teacher might adjust lessons as students progress, divine guidance adapts to humanity's development. Bahá'u'lláh's abrogation of certain laws from the Bayán reflects the unfolding of God's plan, addressing the needs of a more mature global society.

Progressive revelation recognizes the continuity of divine purpose while allowing for change in human understanding and society. Each revelation builds upon the previous ones, refining and expanding spiritual teachings to foster greater unity and understanding. In this way, abrogation becomes a part of a harmonious and continuous divine education rather than a rupture or contradiction.

## Jafar

You're right that themes of spiritual renewal and the anticipation of future messengers are present in earlier religious traditions, including the Qur'an. However, the Bayán was uniquely transformative for its time because it explicitly prepared its followers for the imminent arrival of a new Manifestation of God. The intensity and urgency with which the Báb called for recognition of "He Whom God shall make manifest" were unprecedented. This was not a distant promise but a near event, requiring immediate readiness and a complete reorientation of spiritual life.

One concrete example of Bahá'u'lláh's teachings representing a departure from the Bayán is the law of pilgrimage. The Bayán prescribed pilgrimage to the House of the Báb in Shiraz and other specific sites. Bahá'u'lláh, however, established new sites for pilgrimage, such as the House of Bahá'u'lláh in Baghdad and the Shrine of Bahá'u'lláh in Bahjí. This shift reflects the evolving nature of spiritual guidance, as Bahá'u'lláh's revelation addressed the needs of a global community rather than a localized one.

The concept of progressive revelation encompasses these changes as part of a coherent unfolding of divine will. Each Manifestation brings teachings fit for the age, sometimes requiring radical changes to address humanity's growth. While these changes might seem discontinuous on the surface, they are part of a larger, harmonious plan that gradually leads humanity toward spiritual maturity and unity. This continuity is not always apparent in specific laws but is evident in the overarching goals of justice, unity, and the oneness of humanity.

This principle went beyond spiritual renewal to address social and moral dimensions, advocating for unity across racial, national, and religious boundaries.

Another significant expansion is Bahá'u'lláh's emphasis on consultation as a method for decision-making, contrasting with the more hierarchical structures that might have been implied in earlier laws. This principle encourages collective wisdom and inclusivity, reflecting a maturation of social governance.

These expansions align with the concept of continuity within progressive revelation by building upon the spiritual foundations laid by the Báb while addressing the evolving needs of a global society. Bahá'u'lláh's teachings do not negate the Bayán but rather fulfill and extend its vision, guiding humanity toward greater unity and justice in a way that resonates with the broader trajectory of divine guidance throughout history.
