## You

The Bahá'í calendar is solar, not lunar like the Islamic calendar. What's the rationale, and what does the choice say about the Bahá'í relationship to Islamic tradition?

## Jafar

The Bahá'í calendar, known as the Badí' calendar, is indeed solar, consisting of 19 months each containing 19 days, with the addition of a few intercalary days to match the solar year. This choice marks a departure from the Islamic lunar calendar, which is based on the cycles of the moon.

The use of a solar calendar reflects the Bahá'í Faith's emphasis on a global, unified perspective, transcending specific cultural or religious traditions. A solar calendar aligns more closely with the Gregorian calendar used internationally and is consistent with the Bahá'í principle of embracing a world-embracing vision.

In terms of the relationship to Islamic tradition, the adoption of a solar calendar signifies both continuity and a break. Continuity, because Bahá'u'lláh, the founder of the Bahá'í Faith, was born into a Muslim context and the faith emerged from an Islamic milieu. A break, because the Bahá'í Faith is an independent religion with its own laws and calendar, symbolizing a new cycle of divine revelation. The shift to a solar calendar can be seen as a move towards universalism, emphasizing the faith’s role in addressing the needs of a global civilization.
