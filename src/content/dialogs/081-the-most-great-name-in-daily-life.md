Bahá'ís invoke the Most Great Name (Alláh-u-Abhá) at specific moments — entering a Mashriqu'l-Adhkár, beginning prayer. Why this invocation, and what is being practiced?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The invocation of the Most Great Name, "Alláh-u-Abhá," which means "God the All-Glorious," is a central practice in the Bahá'í Faith. It's more than just a phrase; it's a spiritual exercise that serves to align the heart and mind with the divine presence. By repeating the invocation, Bahá'ís seek to purify their thoughts, focus their intentions, and foster a sense of unity with the will of God.

This invocation is practiced at specific moments, such as entering a Mashriqu'l-Adhkár or beginning prayer, to create a sacred space and a heightened state of awareness. It acts as a reminder of the presence of God and the purpose behind the actions being undertaken. The repetition of the Most Great Name is a way to center oneself, to affirm one's faith, and to connect with the community of believers worldwide, all of whom share this spiritual tradition.

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Bahá'í Faith, invoking the Most Great Name, "Alláh-u-Abhá," is intertwined with spiritual purification and focus. Bahá'u'lláh prescribes the recitation of "Alláh-u-Abhá" ninety-five times daily as part of one's devotion. This practice is meant to prepare the soul, akin to performing ablutions before prayer, to turn towards God with a pure heart and mind:

> "God hath ordained unto him who professeth faith in God, to bathe every day his hands and face, then sit down, turn to God and say: 'Alláh'u'Abhá' ninety-five times." ([*The Most Holy Book (Kitáb-i-Aqdas)*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16712) — Bahá'u'lláh)

This repetition aims to cleanse the mind of distractions and align one's will with the divine, fostering a state of spiritual receptivity and focus.

Comparatively, similar practices exist in other religious traditions. In Hinduism, the repetition of mantras serves a parallel purpose. Mantras are sacred sounds or phrases believed to have spiritual power, used to focus the mind, purify intentions, and connect with the divine. In Orthodox Christianity, the repetition of the Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") functions similarly, cultivating inner peace and divine connection.

All these practices share the goal of spiritual purification and focus, using rhythmic repetition to transcend everyday consciousness and foster a deeper connection with the divine. Though the specific phrases and cultural contexts differ, the underlying intent remains the same across these traditions.
