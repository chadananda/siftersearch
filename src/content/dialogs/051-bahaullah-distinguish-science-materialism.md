### How does Bahá'u'lláh define materialism?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

I've been reading about Bahá'u'lláh's Tablet of Wisdom (Lawh-i-Hikmat). It's striking how it praises philosophy and the natural sciences while also rejecting materialism. How does Bahá'u'lláh draw that line — what does he reject as 'materialism' that's distinct from rejecting science itself?

</div>

#### Bahá'u'lláh distinguishes divine wisdom from materialism.

<div class="jafar-turn">

> "The essence and the fundamentals of philosophy have emanated from the Prophets." ([*Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh Revealed After the Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8270) — Bahá’u’lláh) 

Bahá'u'lláh emphasizes that true knowledge and wisdom originate from divine sources, distinguishing it from materialistic interpretations. He critiques those who misinterpret spiritual truths, suggesting that materialism arises from "the divergence of their views and minds."

#### Science is intertwined with revelation in Bahá'í view.

<div class="jafar-turn">

> "Pure religion ever gives its sanction to true science, for in spirit they are one." ([*Star of the West*](https://siftersearch.com/document/15206) — US Bahá’í News Service)

The relationship between science and revelation in the Bahá'í context suggests that science is not fully autonomous; it is intertwined with spiritual truths. A scientist who disregards revelation may miss the deeper unity that connects their work to the broader spiritual framework.

### How does Bahá'u'lláh define 'nature'?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-6">

#### Nature is the embodiment of God's Name.

<div class="jafar-turn">

> "Nature in its essence is the embodiment of My Name, the Maker, the Creator." ([*Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh Revealed After the Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8270) — Bahá’u’lláh)  
> "Those who have rejected God and firmly cling to Nature as it is in itself are, verily, bereft of knowledge and wisdom." ([*Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh Revealed After the Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8270) — Bahá’u’lláh)  

In these passages, Bahá’u’lláh presents a view of nature that transcends mere physical existence, asserting it as a reflection of divine will. He critiques those who see nature in isolation from God, emphasizing a theological understanding that aligns nature with the Creator.

</div>
