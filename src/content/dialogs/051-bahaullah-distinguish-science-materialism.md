### How does Bahá'u'lláh define materialism?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

I've been reading about Bahá'u'lláh's Tablet of Wisdom (Lawh-i-Hikmat). It's striking how it praises philosophy and the natural sciences while also rejecting materialism. How does Bahá'u'lláh draw that line — what does he reject as 'materialism' that's distinct from rejecting science itself?

</div>

#### Materialism ignores spiritual dimensions, Bahá'u'lláh says.

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Tablet of Wisdom, also known as the Lawh-i-Hikmat, indeed praises the pursuit of philosophy and the natural sciences while rejecting materialism. Bahá'u'lláh draws a distinct line between the two by valuing the sciences and philosophy as means to understand the world and the divine order, while materialism is seen as a focus solely on the physical and neglects spiritual realities. Materialism, in this context, is about reducing existence to mere material components, ignoring the spiritual dimensions that Bahá'u'lláh emphasizes.

He acknowledges the importance of the sciences in advancing human understanding but warns against a worldview that denies the spiritual truths and the deeper reality beyond the physical. This respect for science, coupled with a rejection of materialism, reflects a balanced approach where scientific inquiry is seen as harmonious with spiritual understanding.


</div>

### Does Bahá'u'lláh mention Greek philosophers?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-3">

I just saw you cite this tablet in another conversation. The compilation 'Tablets of Bahá'u'lláh Revealed After the Aqdas' contains it — search for the section on Lawh-i-Hikmat. Does Bahá'u'lláh discuss Greek philosophers there?

</div>

#### Bahá'u'lláh acknowledges Greek philosophers' contributions.

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Tablet of Wisdom, Bahá'u'lláh does mention Greek philosophers. He acknowledges that much of contemporary knowledge and philosophy has its roots in the teachings of these ancient sages. However, he also emphasizes that the foundations of true knowledge come from the Prophets, who have been the ultimate source of wisdom and understanding. The Greek philosophers, in this view, built upon the wisdom initially given by the Prophets. 

This highlights Bahá'u'lláh's respect for both the philosophical contributions of the Greeks and the essential role of divine revelation in advancing human understanding.


</div>

### Which Greek philosophers does he name?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

Names a moment ago: who specifically? Pythagoras? Hippocrates? Plato? Give me the actual passage where he names them.

</div>

#### Bahá'u'lláh names several Greek philosophers explicitly.

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Tablet of Wisdom, Bahá'u'lláh mentions several Greek philosophers, including Hippocrates, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Apollonius of Tyana, and Hermes Trismegistus. He also notes that Empedocles was a contemporary of David and Pythagoras was a contemporary of Solomon. Bahá'u'lláh emphasizes that "the essence and fundamentals of philosophy have emanated from the Prophets." This demonstrates his view that true wisdom and philosophy are rooted in the teachings of the Prophets.


</div>

### What if a scientist ignores revelation?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

That's a striking claim — that philosophy emanates from the Prophets. So science is praised but not as autonomous? What does that imply about a scientist who doesn't acknowledge revelation?

</div>

#### Science is valued but incomplete without spirituality.

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh's view suggests that while science and philosophy are valuable pursuits, they are most complete when they are in harmony with spiritual truths. He emphasizes that the ultimate source of wisdom and understanding is divine revelation. A scientist who does not acknowledge revelation might still contribute greatly to material progress and understanding, but from Bahá'u'lláh's perspective, they might miss the deeper spiritual insights that give fuller meaning to their discoveries.

The implication is not that their work lacks value, but that integrating spiritual wisdom can lead to a more holistic understanding of reality. The sciences are seen as paths that can lead to a greater appreciation of the order and beauty of creation, which, in turn, can point towards the Creator.


</div>

### How does the Tablet define 'nature'?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-6">

Where does the Tablet treat 'nature' specifically? I have a vague recollection that it has a clear theological move — defining nature as something other than what an atheist might assume.

</div>

#### Nature is God's Will, Bahá'u'lláh states.

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Tablet of Wisdom, Bahá'u'lláh defines nature as the embodiment of God's Name, the Maker, the Creator. He describes it as being governed by divine laws and principles, and as an expression of God's Will in the contingent world. Here's a direct passage from the Tablet:

"Say: Nature in its essence is the embodiment of My Name, the Maker, the Creator. Its manifestations are diversified by varying causes, and in this diversity there are signs for men of discernment. Nature is God's Will and is its expression in and through the contingent world." ([*Tablets of Bahá'u'lláh Revealed After the Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/8270) — Bahá'u'lláh)

This framing suggests that nature is not autonomous or random but is a reflection of divine will and order.


</div>

### How does 'nature' affect materialism?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-7">

That's the key move. So 'nature' for Bahá'u'lláh is not raw matter that science studies; it's God's Will manifesting. How does that affect what counts as 'materialism'?

</div>

#### Materialism misses nature's divine dimension.

<div class="jafar-turn">

Exactly, for Bahá'u'lláh, 'nature' being an expression of God's Will means it's not just raw material or mere phenomena. Materialism, then, is seen as the view that limits reality to just those material aspects, ignoring the divine dimension that gives true meaning and purpose to creation.

When nature is viewed as solely physical, devoid of spiritual significance, it can lead to a worldview that misses the interconnectedness of physical and spiritual realities. Bahá'u'lláh's rejection of materialism isn't a rejection of science but rather a call to recognize the spiritual dimensions that science, at its best, can reveal and reflect.


</div>

### What's the historical context of the Tablet?

<div class="user-turn" id="round-8">

What's the historical context — when was the Tablet of Wisdom written and to whom? Knowing what materialism Bahá'u'lláh was actually responding to in the 1880s would help.

</div>

#### Written in 1873, responding to materialist philosophies.

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Tablet of Wisdom (Lawh-i-Hikmat) was written in 1873 in 'Akká to a Bahá'í named Áqá Muhammad. The late 19th century was a time when materialist philosophies were gaining traction in Europe, often characterized by a focus on material progress and the view that reality could be fully explained through physical phenomena alone, without recourse to spiritual or divine explanations.

Bahá'u'lláh was addressing these emerging views, which often saw science as separate from or even opposed to religion. His response in the Tablet of Wisdom is to affirm the value of science and philosophy while also asserting that a complete understanding of reality includes recognition of the divine. The materialism he was responding to was one that denied the spiritual dimensions of existence, which he saw as essential to a true understanding of the universe.


</div>
