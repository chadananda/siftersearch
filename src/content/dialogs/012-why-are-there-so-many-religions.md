### Why many religions if one God

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

If there's one God and one truth, why did God allow so many different religions to develop? Is religious diversity a problem to be resolved, or is it something the texts themselves can explain?

</div>

#### Religious diversity is divinely intended

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, the Bahá'í teachings embrace religious diversity as part of a divine plan rather than a problem. The Báb mentions that "the paths to God are as numerous as the breaths of the creatures," suggesting that diversity in religious expression is natural and intended ([*The Spiritual Journey towards God*](https://siftersearch.com/document/12186)). Bahá'u'lláh reinforces this by saying, "Light is good in whatsoever lamp it is burning," indicating that truth can manifest in various forms and traditions ([*Bahá’í Sacred Writings*](https://siftersearch.com/document/20777)). This perspective encourages us to "be free from prejudice" and appreciate the "Divine light of truth" wherever it appears, whether in Jesus, Moses, or Buddha ([*Bahá’í Sacred Writings*](https://siftersearch.com/document/20777)). So, rather than seeing diversity as a problem, it's viewed as a reflection of the one truth expressed through different lenses.

</div>

### Direct Bahá'u'lláh text on diversity

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Hold on, though — those references sound like interpretations, and the links aren’t to specific texts by Bahá'u'lláh. Where does he himself address this directly? Does the Iqán or another primary text say anything about the purpose of diverse religious paths?

</div>

#### Diverse paths reflect one Truth

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh directly addresses the purpose of diverse religious paths in the *Kitáb-i-Íqán*. He explains that while the "words and utterances" of different religions may "appear to diverge and differ," they are in reality "expressions of one Truth" ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). This diversity is not a problem but a reflection of the "varying capacities and needs of humanity," allowing each person to find their path to God. Bahá'u'lláh also notes how people of "divers beliefs" have been united through the "reviving fragrance of the Divine springtime" ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). So, the differences serve a divine purpose, enriching the spiritual journey rather than hindering it.

</div>

### Tension with latest Manifestation

<div class="user-turn" id="round-3">

Interesting. So Bahá'u'lláh sees religious diversity as a way to reflect humanity's varying needs. But doesn't the Iqán also emphasize the importance of recognizing the latest Manifestation? How does that fit with appreciating older paths? Isn't there a tension there?

</div>

#### Continuity in diversity, no tension

<div class="jafar-turn">

Yeah, Bahá'u'lláh does emphasize the importance of recognizing the "latest Manifestation" in the *Kitáb-i-Íqán*, but he also frames this within the context of a "unified divine plan" ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). He acknowledges that while teachings may "appear to diverge," they ultimately express "one Truth" tailored to different times and capacities ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). This means that appreciating older paths doesn't negate the significance of the latest Revelation; rather, each Manifestation "builds upon the previous ones" ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). So, there's no real tension—it's more about seeing the continuity in the diversity.

</div>

### Rejection of past teachings

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

Wait, but doesn't Bahá'u'lláh also talk about the rejection of past teachings when a new Manifestation comes? If they're all part of one plan, how do you reconcile that with the need to embrace the new and potentially leave old interpretations behind? Where in the Iqán does it lay out how to balance respecting past religions while following the current teachings?

</div>

#### New teachings fulfill past ones

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh indeed addresses the balance between embracing new teachings and respecting past religions in the *Kitáb-i-Íqán*. He explains that all Manifestations are "bearers of one Name" and "exponents of one Cause," highlighting their unity despite apparent differences ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). He also notes that each Manifestation, whether "the Last" or "the First," is essentially "one and the same" because they uphold the same divine Cause ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). This perspective encourages followers to see the new Manifestation as a continuation and fulfillment of past teachings, rather than a rejection.

</div>

### Relevance of past laws and practices

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

Okay, but how does that actually play out in practice? When Bahá'u'lláh talks about "bearers of one Name," is he saying that specific laws and practices from past religions are still relevant, or are they more like spiritual principles that evolve over time? Can you point to a specific section of the Iqán that clarifies this?

</div>

#### Teachings evolve to broader principles

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh in the *Kitáb-i-Íqán* indeed clarifies that while all Manifestations of God are "bearers of His names" and embody divine attributes, the specific laws and practices from past religions may "become darkened" or "exhausted" with the advent of new Revelations ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)). This suggests that the teachings are adapted to the needs of their time, evolving from specific practices to broader spiritual principles. The shift is more about embracing the "light of the latter Revelation" to stay aligned with God's unfolding plan ([*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300)).

</div>
