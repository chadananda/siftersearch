## You

The Bahá'í Faith honors Buddha, Krishna, Christ, Muhammad, and others as Manifestations. Does it honor Zoroaster the same way? Confucius? Mani? Where is the line drawn?

## Jafar

The Bahá'í Faith indeed honors Zoroaster as a Manifestation of God, recognizing Him as part of the same line of divine educators that includes figures like Buddha, Krishna, Christ, and Muhammad. The teachings of Zoroaster are seen as having played a significant role in guiding humanity during His time.

Confucius and Mani, however, are not explicitly mentioned in the Bahá'í writings as Manifestations. The line in the Bahá'í Faith is typically drawn around those who brought a distinct and transformative religious revelation that significantly shaped civilization and had a global impact. The Bahá'í writings emphasize the unity of all religions and the progressive revelation of God's will through these Manifestations. However, not every wise or influential historical figure is necessarily classified as a Manifestation within this framework.

The Bahá'í Faith is open to the possibility that there are other Manifestations not explicitly mentioned in its core texts, acknowledging that God's guidance has reached all peoples, in different forms and contexts, throughout history.

## You

If the Bahá'í Faith recognizes Zoroaster as a Manifestation due to His transformative impact, why are Confucius and Mani not similarly recognized, given their significant influence on vast regions and cultures? Confucianism, for instance, has profoundly shaped Chinese civilization for millennia. What criteria are used to determine the distinction in the case of these figures? Can you point to any specific writings from Bahá'u'lláh or 'Abdu'l-Bahá that clarify this distinction?

## Jafar

In the Bahá'í Faith, Zoroaster is recognized as a Manifestation of God, a status affirmed by Bahá'u'lláh in His writings. For instance, Bahá'u'lláh speaks of Zoroaster with reverence, acknowledging the divine role He played in guiding humanity ([*Responses to Questions of Manakji Sahib*](https://siftersearch.com/document/14978) — Bahá'u'lláh).

Confucius, on the other hand, is not regarded as a Manifestation in the Bahá'í teachings. Shoghi Effendi, the Guardian of the Bahá'í Faith, clarified that Confucius was not a Prophet but rather the founder of a moral system and a great reformer ([*Bahá’í Apocalypticism: The Concept of Progressive Revelation*](https://siftersearch.com/document/14724) — Zaid Lundberg). This distinction is based on the nature and scope of their teachings; Manifestations are believed to bring a new religious law and revelation that significantly transforms society.

The criteria for recognizing someone as a Manifestation in the Bahá'í Faith include the establishment of a new religious system that transforms humanity and aligns with the concept of progressive revelation, where each Manifestation builds upon the teachings of previous ones. While Confucius and Mani significantly influenced their respective cultures, the Bahá'í teachings do not classify them as Manifestations in the same way as Zoroaster or other recognized figures within the Faith.
