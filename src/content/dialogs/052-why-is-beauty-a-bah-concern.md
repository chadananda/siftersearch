The Bahá'í writings repeatedly invoke beauty — the beauty of the world, of a craft, of a building. Why is beauty a religious category in this tradition, when many religious traditions are suspicious of it?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í writings indeed celebrate beauty as a reflection of the divine. Bahá'u'lláh often speaks of the beauty of the natural world and the arts as manifestations of God's attributes. This appreciation for beauty is grounded in the belief that beauty can elevate the human spirit and draw it closer to the divine.

Unlike some traditions that may view beauty with suspicion, fearing it could distract from spiritual pursuits, the Bahá'í perspective sees beauty as harmonizing with the spiritual journey. It serves as a reminder of God's grandeur and creativity. Beauty is not just an aesthetic experience but a spiritual one, helping to cultivate virtues such as love, kindness, and appreciation for creation. In this way, beauty becomes an essential facet of religious life, encouraging a deeper connection to God and fostering a sense of unity and purpose.

Instead, beauty should be appreciated in a way that encourages a deeper understanding and connection to the divine, fostering an appreciation for the Creator rather than the creation alone. This balanced approach allows beauty to play a positive role in spiritual development, aligning with the Bahá'í view of the world as inherently meaningful and divinely orchestrated.
