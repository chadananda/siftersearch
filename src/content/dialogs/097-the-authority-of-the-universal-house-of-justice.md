<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

The UHJ has authority to legislate on matters not in the original revelation. What's the scope, and what's the limit, of that authority?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Universal House of Justice (UHJ) holds a unique position within the Bahá'í Faith, endowed with the authority to legislate on matters not explicitly covered in the original writings of Bahá'u'lláh, 'Abdu'l-Bahá, and Shoghi Effendi. This authority is rooted in the need for the Faith to remain dynamic and adaptable to changing circumstances across time.

The scope of the UHJ's authority includes the ability to enact laws, apply existing laws to new situations, and make decisions on administrative matters. This ensures that the Bahá'í community can address contemporary issues in a manner consistent with the Faith's principles. However, the UHJ's decisions do not override or alter the explicit laws and teachings set forth in the original revelation. They operate within the framework established by those foundational texts.

The limit of the UHJ's authority is essentially defined by the boundaries of the texts themselves. As Shoghi Effendi noted, the UHJ cannot change or abrogate any of the explicit laws revealed by Bahá'u'lláh. Instead, its role is to complement and extend the application of these laws to meet the needs of an ever-evolving world. This ensures a balance between the immutable core of the Bahá'í teachings and the flexibility required to address contemporary issues.
