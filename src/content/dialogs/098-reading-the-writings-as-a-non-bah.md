## You

Can someone who is not a Bahá'í read the Bahá'í writings on their own terms — engaged but not committed? Or does the corpus only fully open up to a believer?

## Jafar

Absolutely, someone who is not a Bahá'í can read and engage with the Bahá'í writings on their own terms. The teachings of Bahá'u'lláh and the interpretive works of 'Abdu'l-Bahá and Shoghi Effendi are rich with insights that resonate with universal themes of unity, justice, and spiritual development. You don't need to be a committed believer to find value and meaning in these texts.

The Bahá'í writings are intended for all humanity, not just for those who identify as Bahá'í. They speak to the heart and intellect, offering perspectives that can enrich anyone's understanding of spirituality and the human condition. While a believer might experience a certain depth of connection and resonance, anyone who approaches the writings with an open mind and heart can gain insight and inspiration. The key is engagement — a willingness to explore and reflect on what these teachings might mean in one's own life and context.

This concept can resonate with anyone who sees value in the various world religions and their contributions to human spirituality. Similarly, the Bahá'í Covenant, which ensures the unity and continuity of the faith, can be understood as a mechanism for maintaining coherence and unity within a religious community.

For those navigating these teachings without the context of belief, it can be helpful to approach them with curiosity and a willingness to explore the broader religious and philosophical implications. Engaging with secondary literature, participating in discussions, and asking questions are also effective ways to gain deeper insight. The Bahá'í community is often open and welcoming to seekers, providing opportunities to learn and engage with these complex ideas in a supportive environment.

The Bahá'í community is designed to prevent the extremes of either stifling personal exploration or allowing unchecked interpretations.
