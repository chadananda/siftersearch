### How should believers treat non-believers

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

What do the religious scriptures say about how believers should treat people who don't share their faith — atheists, people of other religions, skeptics? Is exclusion or condemnation ever the right response according to the texts?

</div>

#### Unity and acceptance emphasized

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh's writings emphasize unity and the importance of seeing beyond differences in belief. He speaks of the "binding force of the Word of God, which uniteth the hearts" of diverse peoples, suggesting that true faith brings people together rather than dividing them [*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Publications/bahaullah_the-kitab-i-iqan#p139). Moreover, Bahá'u'lláh encourages believers to "avoid the land of denial, and advance into the ocean of acceptance," promoting an attitude of openness and understanding towards others [*The Kitáb-i-Íqán*](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Publications/bahaullah_the-kitab-i-iqan#p201). Exclusion or condemnation is not seen as the right response; rather, believers are called to foster unity and acceptance.

</div>

### What do Gospels and Qur'án say

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Hold on — you're only giving me the Bahá'í view. What do the Gospels or the Qur'án say about this? Are there any examples of how believers should treat people of other faiths or skeptics?

</div>

#### Compassion and understanding emphasized

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, the Gospels and the Qur'án also emphasize compassion and understanding towards those of different beliefs. In the Gospels, Jesus teaches to "love your neighbor as yourself," which extends to all people, regardless of their faith. Similarly, the Qur'án advises believers to engage in dialogue with "wisdom and beautiful preaching" and to "argue with them in ways that are best and most gracious" (Qur'án 16:125).

</div>

### Okay, but isn't there also some tension in those…

Okay, but isn't there also some tension in those texts? Like times when exclusion or even harsher responses are mentioned? I'm curious about how those parts are explained.

#### Yeah, there's definitely some tension in the texts regarding exclusion…

<div class="jafar-turn">

Yeah, there's definitely some tension in the texts regarding exclusion or harsher responses. For instance, Bahá'u'lláh mentions that those who hear the verses and persist in ["proud disdain"](https://siftersearch.com/document/8300) are warned of a "painful punishment" — *The Kitáb-i-Íqán*. This highlights a serious consequence for rejecting divine guidance. However, it's important to balance this with the broader emphasis on unity and understanding in the Bahá'í writings, which focus on the importance of seeing beyond differences and fostering compassion. So, while there are warnings about rejection, the overarching message is one of unity and reconciliation.

</div>

### Wait a minute — that still sounds like a…

<div class="user-turn" id="round-4">

Wait a minute — that still sounds like a gloss. Are there explicit parts in the Gospels or the Qur'án where they address exclusion or punishment directly? What do those texts actually say about dealing with non-believers?

#### Actually, the Gospels do address themes of exclusion and judgment…

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, the Gospels do address themes of exclusion and judgment regarding non-believers. In the Gospel of John, Jesus makes a distinction by saying, ["Ye worship ye know not what"](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-john/?paraId=para_118), suggesting a separation between those who understand the truth and those who don't. Furthermore, it mentions that the Spirit will ["reprove the world of sin"](https://siftersearch.com/document/20989), specifically because of disbelief. These passages illustrate a nuanced relationship with non-believers, balancing a call to worship with the consequences of rejecting the truth.

</div>

### Wait, I get that there are consequences mentioned, but what about in…

<div class="user-turn" id="round-5">

Wait, I get that there are consequences mentioned, but what about in the Qur'án? You talked about wisdom and graciousness before. Are there also parts that address punishment or exclusion directly? What's the balance there?

#### The Qur'án indeed addresses both punishment and exclusion directly,…

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Qur'án indeed addresses both punishment and exclusion directly, especially concerning non-believers. It emphasizes that those who reject faith face severe consequences, such as being prepared for Hellfire, while believers are promised rewards. This reflects a balance between divine mercy and justice, urging adherence to faith while acknowledging the consequences of disbelief. Bahá'u'lláh also touches on this theme, noting that those who oppose the truth may be ["tormented and destroyed"](https://oceanlibrary.com/commentary-on-surah-of-sun_bahaullah/?paraId=para_49), serving as a lesson to others. This duality highlights the importance of both wisdom and the reality of divine justice.

</div>
