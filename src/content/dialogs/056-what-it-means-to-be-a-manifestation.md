## You

The Bahá'í Faith calls Bahá'u'lláh, the Báb, Christ, Muhammad, and others 'Manifestations of God.' What ontological category is that, and how is it different from prophet, avatar, or god-man?

## Jafar

The concept of "Manifestations of God" in the Bahá'í Faith is a unique ontological category that signifies divine educators who reflect God's will and attributes to humanity. Unlike the term "prophet," which often implies a messenger conveying God's word without embodying divine qualities, Manifestations are viewed as perfect mirrors reflecting the light of God. They embody both a human and a divine station, serving as conduits for divine revelation.

This differs from the Hindu concept of an "avatar," where a deity incarnates in the world, often with a focus on intervention and preserving dharma. Manifestations of God, while embodying divine attributes, do not claim to be God incarnate but rather His supreme educators.

As for "god-man," which might suggest a literal fusion of divine and human natures as seen in some interpretations of Christ's nature in Christianity, Bahá'í teachings maintain a clear distinction between God and His Manifestations. While they possess a divine station, they are not God Himself but rather His chosen channels to guide humanity.

In essence, Manifestations of God are seen as unique, recurring figures throughout history who bring fresh spiritual teachings suited to the needs of humanity at different times, always reflecting the same divine truth.

## Jafar

Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá describe the Manifestations of God as unique and transcendent beings who reveal the divine will while maintaining a distinct separation from God Himself. Bahá'u'lláh clarifies that there is no direct tie between the one true God and creation, so God ordains that a "pure and stainless Soul" be made manifest to guide humanity:

> "Since there can be no tie of direct intercourse to bind the one True God with His creation, God ordains that in every age and dispensation a pure and stainless Soul be made manifest in the kingdoms of earth and heaven." ([*The Kitáb-i-Aqdas*](https://siftersearch.com/passage/8274) — Bahá’u’lláh)

This indicates a clear distinction between the divine essence and the Manifestations, emphasizing their role as intermediaries rather than incarnations of God. 

Regarding salvation, Bahá'í teachings focus on spiritual education and transformation. The Manifestations guide humanity towards moral and spiritual refinement, rather than emphasizing salvation as a rescue from sin or karma. This approach contrasts with other religious frameworks where salvation might be achieved through faith in a specific prophet or avatar, or through divine intervention. Instead, Bahá'í salvation is about aligning one's life with divine teachings to foster unity and peace.

## Jafar

The Bahá'í Faith addresses the potential divergence from original teachings and the complexities of interpretation through the institution of the Covenant. This framework ensures continuity and unity by designating authoritative interpreters and maintaining a central source of guidance. 'Abdu'l-Bahá, appointed by Bahá'u'lláh as the Center of His Covenant, provided authoritative interpretations during His lifetime. After Him, Shoghi Effendi, as the Guardian, continued this role. Today, the Universal House of Justice, as the supreme governing body, provides ongoing guidance to maintain the unity and integrity of Bahá'í teachings.

This system prevents schisms and ensures that interpretations remain aligned with the original intent by providing a clear channel of authoritative interpretation and guidance. It allows for the adaptation of teachings to address new challenges while safeguarding against misinterpretations.

Regarding the teachings of past Manifestations, the Bahá'í Faith views them as part of a continuous and progressive divine plan. While historical contexts shaped their expression, the core spiritual principles are seen as universal and timeless. The Bahá'í teachings emphasize the need for each age's guidance to address contemporary needs, acknowledging that earlier teachings were tailored to the capacities and conditions of their time. This understanding helps reconcile the potential for teachings to appear as products of their time, highlighting the adaptive and evolving nature of divine revelation. 

Through the Covenant and the institution of the Universal House of Justice, Bahá'í teachings strive to remain relevant and applicable amidst growing cultural and societal complexities, providing a framework for unity and a coherent understanding of spiritual truths across ages.
