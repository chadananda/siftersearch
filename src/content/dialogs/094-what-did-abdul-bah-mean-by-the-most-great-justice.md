<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

'Abdu'l-Bahá speaks of 'the Most Great Justice' as a foundation of the new world order. Is this distributive justice, retributive justice, or something more like a metaphysical justice woven into reality?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

'Abdu'l-Bahá's mention of "the Most Great Justice" indeed goes beyond the typical categorizations of distributive or retributive justice. While those aspects are important and have their place, the justice he envisions is more profound and spiritual, deeply tied to the principles of the Bahá'í Faith.

In the Bahá'í understanding, justice is not merely about fair distribution or punishment; it's about recognizing the oneness of humanity and ensuring that every individual is treated with dignity and respect. This justice is integral to the establishment of a new world order, where the unity of humankind is paramount. It's about creating systems and societies that reflect this unity and the interconnectedness of all people.

So, while "the Most Great Justice" encompasses elements of distributive and retributive justice, it is fundamentally a metaphysical and spiritual principle that serves as a foundation for building a world based on unity, equity, and compassion.

<div class="jafar-turn">

The concept of "the Most Great Justice" as articulated in the Bahá'í writings is deeply entwined with both practical governance and the spiritual fabric of society, emphasizing unity and interconnectedness. Here are some key insights from the writings:

1. **Justice as a Foundational Principle**: Shoghi Effendi highlights that justice is a crowning distinction of Bahá'í institutions, such as Local and National Assemblies, which are precursors to the Universal House of Justice. This underscores the importance of justice in the structure and functioning of Bahá'í governance.

   > "So great and transcendental is this principle of Divine justice, a principle that must be regarded as the crowning distinction of all Local and National Assemblies, in their capacity as forerunners of the Universal House of Justice..." ([*The Advent of Divine Justice*](https://siftersearch.com/passage/8295.59) — Shoghi Effendi)

2. **Justice Beyond Legal Structures**: Bahá’u’lláh's teachings advocate for a vision of society that transcends traditional legal frameworks, focusing on the oneness of humanity and a global ethic. This vision includes democratic governance, human rights, and social justice as inseparable from global citizenship.

   > "Bahá’u’lláh articulated an organic theory of society, but one in which all humanity is defined as that organic whole--not simply the nation-state." ([*Logos and Civilization: Spirit, History, and Order in the Writings of Bahá'u'lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/passage/7165.33) — Nader Saiedi)

3. **Unity Through Justice**: The purpose of justice is seen as a precondition for true unity, where governance involves international law and the elimination of prejudices.

   > "...the purpose of justice is understood to be a precondition for the appearance of true unity." ([*Bahá’í: History, Beliefs, Practices, Theological Exchanges, and Current Issues*](https://siftersearch.com/passage/13849.30) — Christopher Buck)

These passages illustrate how Bahá'í teachings envision justice as a transformative principle that goes beyond mere legal structures, aiming to foster a unified and equitable global society.

   > "The purpose of justice is the appearance of unity among men. The ocean of divine wisdom surgeth within this exalted word whilst the books of the world cannot contain its inner significance." ([*Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/passage/8466.6) — Bahá’u’lláh)

3. **Role Within Assemblies**: Shoghi Effendi's emphasis on justice as a crowning distinction of Bahá'í institutions underlines the collective responsibility of individuals in these bodies. Members of Assemblies are called to act with justice, free from personal biases, ensuring decisions reflect the unity and well-being of the community.

Individual responsibility in achieving justice involves living by these principles in both personal and collective contexts. This means practicing fairness, seeking truth independently, and contributing to communal harmony through just actions and decisions. Through these efforts, individuals play a crucial role in realizing the higher form of justice envisioned by Bahá'í teachings.
