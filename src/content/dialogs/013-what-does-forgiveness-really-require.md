### Is forgiveness unconditional in religion

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

Forgiveness is central to nearly every religious tradition. But is it unconditional? Does the offender need to repent? Can you forgive someone who doesn't acknowledge the harm? What do the texts actually say about the conditions for forgiveness?

</div>

#### Forgiveness linked to repentance

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Báb's writings suggest that forgiveness is closely tied to repentance. He mentions that forgiveness is granted to "those who have repented and returned to the truth," indicating that a change of heart or acknowledgment of wrongdoing plays a role in divine forgiveness ([*Súratu'l-Mulk*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16306)). This implies that while forgiveness is a divine attribute, it may not be entirely unconditional, as it seems to be linked to the offender's willingness to "repent and believe" ([*Súratu'l-Mulk*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16306)). So, in this context, forgiveness appears to require some form of acknowledgment or repentance from the offender.

</div>

### Personal forgiveness without repentance

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Interesting, but what about when it comes to personal forgiveness between people? Does Bahá'u'lláh or 'Abdu'l-Bahá say anything about forgiving someone who hasn't repented or acknowledged their harm?

</div>

#### Forgiveness not dependent on repentance

<div class="jafar-turn">

When it comes to personal forgiveness, Bahá'í teachings emphasize a spirit of forgiveness that doesn't necessarily depend on the other person's repentance. While the Báb mentions that forgiveness is granted to "those who have repented and returned to the truth," this doesn't mean forgiveness is withheld until the other acknowledges their harm ([*Súratu'l-Mulk*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16306)). 'Abdu'l-Bahá encourages us to forgive others as a reflection of divine mercy, suggesting that forgiveness can be an act of personal liberation and spiritual growth, regardless of the offender's actions. This approach aligns with the broader Bahá'í principle of fostering unity and love among all people.

</div>

#### Forgiveness in personal relationships

<div class="jafar-turn">

'Abdu'l-Bahá does address forgiveness in "Some Answered Questions," particularly in the context of personal relationships. He notes that "if one person assaults another, the injured party should forgive" ([*Some Answered Questions*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8346)). However, he also emphasizes that forgiveness in personal matters doesn't mean ignoring justice in broader societal contexts. This suggests a balance where personal forgiveness is encouraged, but it doesn't negate the need for justice and accountability in public affairs.

</div>
