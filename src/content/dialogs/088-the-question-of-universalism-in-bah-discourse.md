## You

Is the Bahá'í Faith universalist in the strong sense — that all paths reach God — or only in the weaker sense that all paths share a common root? The distinction matters.

## Jafar

The Bahá'í Faith holds a nuanced position that might be seen as a "strong universalism" in its emphasis on the unity of all religions. Bahá'ís believe that all major world religions come from the same divine source and are part of a single unfolding plan, each suited to the time and place of its revelation. This suggests more than just a common root; it implies that each path is a legitimate and necessary expression of humanity's relationship with the divine.

However, the Bahá'í Faith also teaches that while these paths share a divine origin, they are not identical and may not lead to the same spiritual destination in every detail or practice. Each has its own unique contribution to make to the spiritual evolution of humanity. In this sense, there's an acknowledgment of diversity within unity. So, it's not that all paths are interchangeable, but rather that they are different stages in a continuous journey toward God. This perspective seeks to harmonize the truths found in all religions rather than asserting that they are all equivalent in every aspect.

## Jafar

The Bahá'í Faith indeed presents a challenging balance between unity and uniqueness. Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá emphasized the oneness of all religions and humanity, recognizing the divine origin of each major faith. However, they also introduced the concept of progressive revelation, suggesting that God's guidance is revealed progressively over time through different messengers, each suited to the needs of their age.

For example, 'Abdu'l-Bahá acknowledged that past religious systems often claimed superiority over others, leading to division. He emphasized teachings conducive to fellowship and unity, urging believers to move beyond exclusivity. The writings propose a framework where each religion has its own role and contribution to the spiritual evolution of humanity.

> "The first form of dissension arises from religious differences. Bahá’u’lláh has given full teachings to the world which are conducive to fellowship and unity in religion." ([H.M. Balyuzi, *’Abdu’l-Bahá -- The Centre of the Covenant of Bahá’u’lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/passage?id=3789))

This does mean that the Bahá'í Faith views itself as part of a continuous divine plan, which may seem at odds with religions that see themselves as final revelations. However, the Bahá'í perspective respects the independent claims of each faith while suggesting a broader, unifying purpose. It's a vision that invites dialogue and understanding without forcing uniformity.

Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá engage with the Islamic concept of Muhammad as the "Seal of the Prophets" by interpreting it in a way that respects its traditional significance while also asserting the continuity of divine revelation. In the Bahá'í understanding, the "seal" signifies the completion of a prophetic cycle but not the cessation of divine guidance. Bahá'u'lláh presents his own revelation as inaugurating a new cycle, not contradicting or replacing the truths revealed by Muhammad.

Bahá'u'lláh addresses this directly in several writings, acknowledging the Qur'anic verse that refers to Muhammad as the "Seal of the Prophets." He suggests that while the station of prophecy was sealed with Muhammad, the divine promise of further guidance remains, fulfilling humanity's ongoing spiritual needs.

> "For God, blessed and exalted may he be, sealed the station of prophecy in regard to his Beloved, his intimate Friend, the most excellent of his Creatures [Muhammad]. Thus was it revealed in the Realm of Grandeur: \"But he is the messenger of God and the seal of the prophets.\"[Qur'an 33:40] Afterwards, he promised mankind that they would attain to the divine Presence..." ([Gems of the Mysteries (Jawáhir al-Asrár)](https://siftersearch.com/passage?id=15039) — Bahá'u'lláh)

This approach allows Bahá'ís to honor the finality of Muhammad's prophetic mission within its own cycle while introducing the idea that divine revelation continues in new forms. It respects the authority of previous religions by acknowledging their fulfillment in their own context and by suggesting that renewal does not diminish their truth. This balance aims to foster respect and dialogue among diverse faiths while affirming the Bahá'í vision of progressive revelation.
