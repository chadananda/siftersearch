## You

How do the Bahá'í writings handle anger? Is it a passion to be suppressed, transmuted, or expressed?

## Jafar

In the Bahá'í writings, anger is generally seen as a passion that should be transformed rather than merely suppressed or expressed. 'Abdu'l-Bahá, in "The Secret of Divine Civilization," implies a negative view of anger by associating it with idleness and blindness, suggesting that an individual prone to anger is like "a morsel of flesh without eyes." This metaphor might imply that anger can cloud one's judgment and perception, preventing spiritual insight.

The Bahá'í teachings encourage individuals to cultivate virtues such as patience, compassion, and understanding, which can help transform the energy of anger into more constructive forms. The emphasis is on education and spiritual development to guide individuals towards a state of greater harmony and insight.

## Jafar

The Bahá'í writings do acknowledge that emotions like anger can arise from witnessing injustice, and they recognize the importance of responding to such situations. However, the guidance emphasizes channeling that energy constructively rather than allowing anger to dominate one's actions or cloud judgment.

'Abdu'l-Bahá and other Bahá'í texts encourage responding to injustice with qualities like justice, wisdom, and compassion. The focus is on constructive action, such as striving for justice and working towards societal change with a spirit of love and understanding. While the writings may not explicitly describe anger as "righteous," they do recognize the importance of standing against injustice and working for positive change. The key is to respond with a balanced and thoughtful approach, ensuring that actions are guided by spiritual principles rather than reactive emotions.

So, while anger itself is often seen as a condition to be transcended or transformed, the underlying motivation to address injustice is acknowledged and supported, provided it is expressed in ways that align with Bahá'í principles of unity and compassion.
