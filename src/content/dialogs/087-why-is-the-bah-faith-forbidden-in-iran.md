## You

The Bahá'í Faith is illegal in Iran, the country of its origin. What's the actual basis of the Iranian state's persecution, and why have Bahá'ís remained committed to the country?

## Jafar

The persecution of Bahá'ís in Iran is rooted in a complex mix of religious, political, and historical factors. The Bahá'í Faith emerged in the 19th century in Persia, now Iran, in a predominantly Shi'a Muslim context. The faith's foundational principles, such as the oneness of humanity, the unity of religions, and the progressive revelation of religious truth, challenge traditional clerical authority and interpretations. This has historically provoked suspicion and hostility from religious leaders who see the Bahá'í Faith as a heretical deviation from Islam.

Politically, the Bahá'ís have been seen as a convenient scapegoat for various social and political issues. The Bahá'í Faith's emphasis on global unity and its lack of allegiance to any national or political ideology have led to accusations of foreign influence or espionage, despite the community's consistent commitment to nonviolence and nonpartisanship.

Despite the severe persecution, including discrimination, imprisonment, and denial of basic rights, Bahá'ís have remained committed to Iran as it is their spiritual homeland. Their commitment is also driven by the principle of service to humanity, which includes contributing positively to society wherever they are. Bahá'ís believe in the transformative power of enduring adversity with dignity and hope that their perseverance can eventually lead to greater understanding and unity in Iran.

## Jafar

The challenge to clerical authority by the Bahá'í Faith can be traced back to its origins with the Báb, whose teachings directly threatened the established power of the Muslim clergy. His message not only challenged traditional Shí‘ah Islam but also proposed revolutionary social changes that were alarming to the clerical establishment. This inherent challenge is reflected in several Bahá'í writings:

> "In the case of the Báb's claims, there was another crucial aspect: His message not only challenged the foundations of traditional Shí‘ah Islam as a set of beliefs, but more specifically it directly threatened the basis of the Muslim clergy's power and authority." (Nader Saiedi, [*Gate of the Heart: Understanding the Writings of the Báb*](https://siftersearch.com/document/8632))

Regarding the accusations of foreign influence, there have been instances where the Iranian state and others have propagated narratives suggesting that Bahá'ís are aligned with foreign powers or political movements. This has often been a tactic to delegitimize the Bahá'í community and justify persecution:

> "You wrote that it had been stated in the _Hablu'l-Matín_ published at Rasht that the Bahá'ís were partisans of the Autocracy, and at Zanján had collected aid for the Royalist Cause. One of the 'Friends' must write to some other newspaper, or it must be spread abroad amongst the people, that this is a calumny concerning the Bahá'ís..." (Abdu'l-Bahá, [*Attitude of Bahá’ís towards Persian Politics*](https://siftersearch.com/document/16590))

Despite these challenges, Bahá'ís have consistently sought to contribute positively to society through acts of service and community building. Even in a hostile environment like Iran, Bahá'ís remain committed to education, healthcare, and the promotion of ethical standards. Their resilience and dedication to these principles can be seen in efforts to maintain underground educational initiatives, like the Bahá'í Institute for Higher Education, which provides learning opportunities for Bahá'í youth who are barred from attending university. The spirit of service is deeply ingrained in the Bahá'í ethos, and this commitment continues even in adversity.

The Bahá'í teaching of progressive revelation presents a significant challenge to traditional clerical authority because it suggests that religious truth is not static but unfolds progressively through a series of divinely inspired messengers. This concept implies that no single religious tradition holds a monopoly on truth, which undermines the exclusive interpretative power often claimed by clerical establishments. Bahá'u'lláh's declaration that his revelation is part of this ongoing process poses a direct theological challenge to the finality of previous prophetic dispensations, including Islam's view of Muhammad as the "Seal of the Prophets."

A particularly contentious aspect is the Bahá'í belief that divine guidance continues beyond Muhammad, without the need for a clergy to mediate between God and believers. This democratization of spiritual authority is seen as a threat to clerical structures that rely on hierarchical power and control over religious interpretation.

On the topic of foreign influence, the Iranian authorities have, in recent decades, frequently portrayed the Bahá'í community as collaborators with foreign powers, particularly Western governments and Israel. These narratives are perpetuated through state media and are often unfounded. The Bahá'í International Community consistently responds to such claims by reiterating their principles of political neutrality and loyalty to their respective countries while seeking international support to highlight the baselessness of these accusations.

Regarding the Bahá'í Institute for Higher Education (BIHE), it operates as an informal educational network, providing courses through online platforms and in-person classes held discreetly in private homes. Despite facing government raids, arrests, and confiscation of materials, the BIHE has persisted through the dedication of volunteers and the ingenuity of its students and faculty. They often rely on support from international academic institutions that recognize BIHE credits, enabling students to continue their education abroad. The resilience of the BIHE demonstrates the community's commitment to education and empowerment despite systemic oppression.
