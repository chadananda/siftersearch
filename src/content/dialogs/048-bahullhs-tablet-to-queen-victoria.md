In the Tablet to Queen Victoria, Bahá'u'lláh praises her for abolishing the trade in slaves and entrusting the governance to the representatives of the people, which He sees as conducive to the stability and tranquility of her realm. He encourages her to turn her heart toward God and to recognize the Manifestation of God in this age. Bahá'u'lláh calls for the union of all people in one universal Cause and one common Faith as the remedy for the world's ills, emphasizing that this can only be achieved under the guidance of a divinely inspired Physician. He urges the members of assemblies to counsel together for the betterment of mankind, likening the world to a sick body in need of healing through unity and divine guidance.

He advocates for a global order based on justice and collective security, where leaders and nations consult for the common good. Bahá'u'lláh acknowledges the complexities and potential conflicts inherent in this vision, but He emphasizes that these challenges can be managed through adherence to divine guidance and principles such as consultation, moderation, and justice.

His teachings offer a framework for reconciling existing power structures by encouraging leaders to act as trustees for the well-being of their people, promoting policies that foster unity and cooperation rather than division and conflict.

Bahá'u'lláh envisions the mechanisms of consultation and justice as being put into practice through the establishment of just institutions, such as the House of Justice, which are tasked with legislating laws that reflect divine wisdom and are responsive to the needs of the time. He acknowledges the challenges of implementing such changes, especially amidst resistance from established authorities. He suggests that rulers should act with magnanimity and justice, ensuring that their policies serve the common good and align with divine ordinances, which have historically satisfied and brought peace to diverse peoples.

> "To put in force the ordinances of God is unmixed justice, and with this all are satisfied; nay, the ordinances of God have been and will ever be a cause of unity and harmony among men." ([Tablet to Nasiri'd Din Shah (Lawh-i-Sultan)](https://siftersearch.com/document/16700) — Bahá'u'lláh)

The call is for a gradual but persistent transformation, with the understanding that true and lasting change is rooted in spiritual principles and collective action.
