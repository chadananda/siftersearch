## You

Bahá'u'lláh's Tablet of Carmel addresses Mount Carmel as a person and prophesies its becoming a center of pilgrimage. How should a 21st-century reader receive this addressing of a mountain?

## Jafar

When Bahá'u'lláh addresses Mount Carmel as if it were a person in the Tablet of Carmel, he uses a literary device known as apostrophe, which is common in poetic and scriptural traditions. By personifying the mountain, Bahá'u'lláh imbues it with spiritual significance, transforming it from a mere geographical location into a symbol of divine revelation and promise. In the 21st century, readers can appreciate this not as an archaic style but as a way of expressing profound truths about the sacredness of the land and its role in the unfolding of the Bahá'í Faith.

Mount Carmel is not just a physical place; it is a symbol of the covenant and the center of spiritual renewal. The prophecy of it becoming a center of pilgrimage has indeed been realized, as the Bahá'í World Centre and the shrines of the Báb and Bahá'u'lláh are located there. For modern readers, this personification invites reflection on how physical spaces can carry spiritual and historical significance, reminding us of the ongoing relationship between the divine and the material world.
