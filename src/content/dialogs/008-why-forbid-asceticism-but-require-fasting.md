### Tension between fasting and monasticism

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

Bahá'u'lláh forbids monasticism and ascetic withdrawal from the world, yet requires a 19-day fast. Aren't these in tension? How is one a discipline and the other an excess?

</div>

#### Different spiritual purposes explained

<div class="jafar-turn">

Bahá'u'lláh's teachings on monasticism and fasting might seem contradictory at first, but they serve different spiritual purposes. Monasticism, with its withdrawal from the world, is discouraged because believers are encouraged to ["not deprive yourselves of His gifts"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/bahaullah_tablet-of-the-temple-surih-i-haykal-2#p150), *Tablet of the Temple*, and to engage with the world in a balanced way. In contrast, the 19-day fast is a temporary and focused period of spiritual discipline. Bahá'u'lláh describes it as a time to ["forsake all that ye possess and hold fast unto that which God hath purposed"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Publications/bahaullah_summons-of-the-lord-of-hosts#p164), *Summons of the Lord of Hosts*. This fasting is not about permanent withdrawal but about renewing one's spiritual focus and cleansing the soul from worldly distractions.

</div>

### Comparison with Islam and Buddhism

<div class="user-turn" id="round-2">

Okay, I get how the fast is more like a temporary discipline than a permanent withdrawal. But what about other traditions? Like, does Islam see fasting in the same way? Or how does Buddhism approach ascetic practices? Are they saying something different about withdrawal and discipline?

</div>

#### Different traditions' views on discipline

<div class="jafar-turn">

In Islam, fasting during Ramadan is seen as a time to "increase spiritual tendencies" and "reduce carnal inclinations," much like Bahá'u'lláh describes in the *Tablet on the Fast* [Bahá'u'lláh](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/bahaullah_tablet-on-the-fast-2#p1). It is a discipline that transforms one's focus from the material to the spiritual. In Buddhism, ascetic practices are part of a broader path to enlightenment, where the Buddha taught that moderation is key — avoiding both self-indulgence and extreme self-denial. The Buddhist path emphasizes the [Four Noble Truths](https://siftersearch.com/library/Buddhist/Pali%20Canon/tr-sister-vajira_digha-nikaya-16-mahaparinibbana-sutta-last-days-of-the-buddha#p113), which guide practitioners to understand suffering and the path to its cessation. Each tradition, while differing in specifics, uses discipline as a means to deepen spiritual awareness and growth.

</div>

#### General references to Qur'an and Buddhism

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Qur'an, fasting is discussed as a means to cultivate self-discipline and spiritual awareness. It states, ["Fasting is prescribed for you as it was prescribed for those before you, that you may become righteous"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/abdul-baha_mysterious-forces-of-civilization-secret-of-divine-civilization#p744). This highlights fasting as a practice intended to foster righteousness and self-control. 

Regarding Buddhism, the concept of the Middle Way, which advocates for a balanced approach to life, is central. While the retrieved quotes don't directly address fasting, the Middle Way itself is about avoiding extremes, including excessive asceticism. If you want to explore more, the Pali Canon, particularly the *Dhammapada*, often discusses these themes.

This verse highlights fasting as a means to develop piety and self-discipline. #### Dhammapada passage on balance

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the *Dhammapada*, the idea of the Middle Way is beautifully captured in the advice to ["give up what is before, give up what is behind, give up what is in the middle"](https://siftersearch.com/document/20925), suggesting a release from all attachments to reach liberation. This passage highlights the balance between excess and discipline, urging one to let go of desires and attachments to achieve a state of freedom and not be bound by the cycle of birth and decay. This reflects the Buddhist emphasis on finding a path that avoids extremes, embodying the essence of the Middle Way.
