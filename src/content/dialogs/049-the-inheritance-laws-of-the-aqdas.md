The Kitáb-i-Aqdas contains detailed inheritance laws apportioning wealth among various relatives in fixed fractions. They look ancient. What's the intent, and how does this fit with the Bahá'í teaching of equality?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The inheritance laws in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas indeed reflect a structure that might seem ancient or rigid at first glance, but they hold a deeper purpose within the Bahá'í Faith. These laws were revealed by Bahá'u'lláh in a time and place where such detailed prescriptions were necessary to bring about justice and order in societies that lacked systematic inheritance rules.

The intent behind these laws is to provide a clear, divinely ordained structure that ensures fairness and minimizes disputes among family members after a person's passing. It also reflects a transition from previous religious systems, preserving continuity while establishing new principles for an evolving society.

While the laws prescribe specific shares to family members, they also allow for some flexibility. For example, Bahá'ís can write a will to distribute up to a third of their estate differently, which can accommodate personal wishes and circumstances. This reflects the Bahá'í teaching that balances divine law with individual responsibility and choice.

In terms of equality, the Bahá'í Faith teaches the equality of men and women and stresses the importance of justice. The inheritance laws are part of a broader framework that seeks to establish societal harmony and address the needs and rights of individuals within the family and community context. The Universal House of Justice, the supreme governing body of the Bahá'í Faith, is empowered to adjust or adapt these laws as society evolves, ensuring that the principles of justice and equality remain central.
