Why is alcohol categorically forbidden in the Bahá'í Faith, but tobacco only strongly discouraged rather than forbidden? Both are intoxicants of a kind. The asymmetry is not arbitrary — what's the textual reasoning?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í teachings categorically forbid alcohol because it "leads the mind astray and is the cause of weakening the body." This prohibition is clearly stated in the Book of Aqdas, reflecting the belief that alcohol's intoxicating effects can impair judgment and spiritual perception, leading one away from their spiritual path. 

> "Regarding the use of liquors: According to the texts of the Book of Akdas, both light and strong drinks are prohibited. The reason for this prohibition is that it leads the mind astray and is the cause of weakening the body." ([Tablets of Bahá’u’lláh and ’Abdu’l-Bahá from Star of the West](https://siftersearch.com))

On the other hand, tobacco is strongly discouraged rather than outright forbidden. The primary reasons given are its harmful health effects and its nature as a "dirty, smelly, offensive" habit. Though the Báb initially prohibited tobacco, the Bahá'í teachings have not maintained an absolute ban in the same way as with alcohol. Instead, the harmfulness of tobacco is emphasized and its use is discouraged from a hygienic and health perspective.

> "Among the latter is smoking tobacco, which is dirty, smelly, offensive -- an evil habit, and one the harmfulness of which gradually becometh apparent to all." ([Crown of Glory: Memoirs of Jinab-i-Aziz'u'llah Azizi](https://siftersearch.com))

The distinction seems to arise from the different impacts these substances are perceived to have on one's mental and spiritual faculties, with alcohol being seen as a direct impediment to spiritual clarity.
