<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas contains laws — some unenforced, some applied. How should a Bahá'í today understand the laws as a whole — living law, aspirational vision, or both at once?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas, often referred to as the Most Holy Book, serves as both a body of living law and an aspirational vision for Bahá'ís. It is a dynamic document that outlines the spiritual and social framework for a future global civilization, while also providing practical laws for individual and community life.

The laws in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas are not all meant to be enforced immediately. Some are explicitly intended for a future time when society is better prepared to implement them, and others are left to the discretion of the Universal House of Justice to apply as circumstances evolve. This reflects the Bahá'í understanding that humanity is on a spiritual and social journey that unfolds progressively.

At the same time, the laws offer an aspirational vision of the oneness of humanity, justice, and unity. They guide Bahá'ís in striving towards personal and collective ideals that align with these principles. In this way, the Kitáb-i-Aqdas is both a practical guide and a source of inspiration, encouraging believers to live in accordance with its teachings while understanding that the full implementation of its laws is a gradual process.

</div>

The concept of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas as a "living law" inherently involves a dynamic relationship between the laws that are immediately binding and those that are aspirational or intended for future implementation. This does not create a disconnect but rather reflects the Bahá'í understanding of progressive revelation and the gradual maturation of society.

The Universal House of Justice, as the supreme legislative body of the Bahá'í Faith, plays a crucial role in this process. It is tasked with applying the laws of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas in a way that considers the current capacity and conditions of the Bahá'í community and the wider world. This means that some laws are enacted when the community is ready, while others are held in abeyance until society reaches the necessary level of development.

For example, certain social laws, such as those related to the administration of justice or specific aspects of personal conduct, may be deferred because the necessary societal structures or understandings are not yet in place. Meanwhile, principles like the oneness of humanity and the importance of universal education are emphasized and actively pursued as foundational to community life.

The Universal House of Justice has approached this issue by providing guidance on the application of laws and by fostering the development of the Bahá'í community in ways that prepare it for future responsibilities. This approach ensures that the laws of Bahá'u'lláh remain relevant and applicable, even when some are not immediately enforced.

Living with this tension involves recognizing that the Kitáb-i-Aqdas is both a guide for present action and a blueprint for future development. Bahá'ís are encouraged to strive towards the ideals set forth in the book, while also being patient and understanding of the gradual nature of societal transformation. This dynamic process is central to the Bahá'í approach to law and spiritual growth.
