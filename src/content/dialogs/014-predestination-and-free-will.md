### Bahá'í view on predestination and free will

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

How do the Bahá'í writings handle the ancient question of predestination and free will? They seem to affirm both — but on what terms?

</div>

#### Affirms both predestination and free will

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í writings indeed grapple with the tension between predestination and free will, affirming both in a nuanced manner. Bahá'u'lláh addresses this by acknowledging God's omnipotence and omniscience, questioning whether we truly have ["independent volition"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Tablet%20Translations/bahaullah_the-city-of-divine-unity#p13) or if our choices are preordained. He suggests that while God's knowledge encompasses all, humans still possess a degree of free will to make choices within that divine framework. The Báb also reflects this balance, noting that ["whatever you will, whenever you will, has already been decreed"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Tablets/the-bab_kitabur-ruh_ar#p161), yet this does not negate the role of human agency. This dual affirmation allows for a harmonious coexistence of divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

</div>

#### Explains balance with Báb's words

<div class="jafar-turn">

Certainly! The Báb addresses the balance between predestination and free will by describing a "station of the loss of the unity of actions," where many stumble due to ignorance of "the secret of destiny and the decree of the ordained." He notes that some lean towards "compulsion" and others towards "delegation," both of which he considers losses. The true understanding lies in observing "the matter between the two matters" and recognizing the "stations of the choices of all" as they truly are, which involves seeing "the decree of destiny, the secret of the ordained" in every affair [*Tafsír-i-Súriy-i-'Aṣr*](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Tablets/098-Tafsir-i-Suriy-i-Asr#p53).

</div>

### Direct quotes from Bahá'í and others

#### Provides Báb's quote and others

<div class="jafar-turn">

Certainly! Here's a direct quote from the Báb: "Follow the command of your Lord and do not fear anyone, for all shall return to Us. And We will judge on the Day of Resurrection among all with justice" [*Kitábu'r-Rúḥ*](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Tablets/the-bab_kitabur-ruh_ar#p36). This passage reflects the balance between divine decree and human response.

In Christianity, Jesus speaks to the interplay of divine will and human action when he says, "Your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven", *Gospel of Matthew*. Similarly, the Qur'án acknowledges human agency within divine sovereignty: "Indeed, Allah will not change the condition of a people until they change what is in themselves", *Surah Ar-Ra'd*. Each tradition offers a unique perspective on this profound question.

</div>

#### Discusses Gospels and Qur'án

<div class="jafar-turn">

In the Gospels, human agency is indeed a significant theme. For instance, Jesus emphasizes the importance of choice and action when he says, ["the kingdom of God is within you"](https://oceanlibrary.com/kjv-gospel-of-luke/?paraId=para_846), suggesting that individuals have the power to engage with the divine through their own decisions. Meanwhile, in the Qur'án, the verse about "no compulsion in religion" fits into a broader dialogue about destiny and free will by affirming that faith must be a personal choice, not coerced, aligning with the idea that ["nothing will befall us except what Allah has decreed for us"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Tablets/the-bab_suratul-mulk_fa#p1500), acknowledging both divine sovereignty and human responsibility. These scriptures collectively highlight a balance where divine will and human agency coexist, inviting believers to actively participate in their spiritual journeys.
