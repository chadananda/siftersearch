## You

How do the Bahá'í writings handle the ancient question of predestination and free will? They seem to affirm both — but on what terms?

## Jafar

The Bahá'í writings embrace both the concepts of predestination and free will, but they do so in a way that transcends the typical binary opposition. Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá explain that while God's will is ultimately sovereign and all-encompassing, human beings are endowed with the capacity to make choices and exercise free will within the framework of divine laws.

'Abdu'l-Bahá likens this relationship to a ship on the ocean: the ocean represents the vast, immutable will of God, while the ship's captain represents human free will. The captain has the freedom to navigate the ship as he wishes, but his choices are always subject to the natural laws and conditions of the ocean. This metaphor illustrates how human freedom operates within the bounds of divine will. 

In this view, the apparent paradox is resolved by understanding that God's knowledge and will do not negate human agency but rather encompass it in a way that allows for both divine omniscience and genuine human choice. This approach echoes the unity of seemingly opposing forces, a recurring theme in Bahá'í thought.

The Bahá'í writings address the tension between predestination and free will, especially in the context of actions that defy moral or spiritual laws. 'Abdu'l-Bahá explains that while God's will is all-encompassing, human beings have been granted the gift of free will to make choices. This capacity for choice means that individuals can act in ways that align with or oppose divine teachings. When actions such as injustice or evil occur, they are not part of God's will but rather a misuse of the free will granted to humans.

Shoghi Effendi touches on this in *Bahá’í Administration*, where he notes the existence of divine destiny alongside human frailty. The presence of injustice and evil in the world is seen as a consequence of human choice, not an indication of God's will. Instead, these actions highlight the responsibility that comes with free will — each person is accountable for their actions and their alignment with divine guidance. The Bahá'í view suggests that while God's will sets the framework within which we operate, it is up to individuals to choose how they navigate that framework.

'Abdu'l-Bahá, in *Some Answered Questions*, describes how the choice between good and evil belongs to humans, yet all actions occur within the sovereignty of God:

> "Our meaning is that the choice of good and evil belongs to man, but that under all circumstances he is dependent upon the life-sustaining assistance of Divine Providence. The sovereignty of God is great indeed, and all are held captive in the grasp of His power. The servant can do nothing of his own will alone: God is almighty and all-powerful and bestows His assistance upon all creation." ([*Some Answered Questions*](https://siftersearch.com/8346))

This passage suggests that while human beings have the autonomy to choose, their actions are still under the overarching sovereignty of God. This doesn't mean that evil is part of God's will, but rather that God allows for human choice, even when it results in actions contrary to divine teachings. The existence of injustice and evil is thus framed as a consequence of human agency rather than divine intent.

In terms of divine justice, Bahá'í teachings hold that while suffering may occur due to others' misuse of free will, ultimate justice is served in the spiritual realm. The material world is seen as a place of testing and growth, where the soul's true nature is revealed through its responses to life's challenges. This perspective emphasizes the eternal nature of the soul and the ultimate justice and mercy of God that transcends temporal suffering.
