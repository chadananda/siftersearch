## You

The Bahá'í Faith calls racism America's 'most challenging issue.' What is the structure of the teaching — is racism understood as a sin, a sickness, a structural condition, or all of these?

## Jafar

The Bahá'í teachings indeed describe racism as America's "most challenging issue," reflecting the deep concern for racial unity and justice. Racism in the Bahá'í perspective is understood through several lenses. It can be seen as a moral failing or a "sin" because it violates the fundamental spiritual principle of the oneness of humanity. Bahá'u'lláh's writings emphasize that all humans are created from the same substance and should regard each other as members of one family.

Additionally, racism is described as a kind of sickness, a disease that afflicts both the individual and society. This metaphor highlights the need for healing, transformation, and education to overcome prejudices and create a healthy, unified community.

From a structural standpoint, racism is recognized as embedded within social, economic, and political systems. It requires not only personal change but also sweeping societal reforms to dismantle discriminatory structures and practices.

The Bahá'í approach to overcoming racism involves fostering unity through education, dialogue, and concerted community action, aiming to create an environment where every individual can thrive regardless of race. It's a comprehensive approach that addresses personal attitudes, community dynamics, and institutional reforms.

## Jafar

The Bahá'í teachings address racism through a comprehensive approach, involving moral, medicinal, and structural dimensions, with guidance from figures like 'Abdu'l-Bahá and Shoghi Effendi. Here are some specific approaches:

1. **Moral and Spiritual Transformation**: The Bahá'í writings emphasize the importance of personal transformation and the elimination of prejudice from the hearts of individuals. 'Abdu'l-Bahá, during his travels in America, repeatedly stressed the need for love and unity among all races. He warned that failing to address racial prejudice could lead to increased enmity and even bloodshed:

   > "Although working endlessly to promote racial unity through inspiring talks and actions, 'Abdu'l-Bahá understood the persistent reality of racism in the U.S. In a letter to a Chicago Bahá'í, He predicted what would happen if racial attitudes did not change: 'Enmity will be increased day by day and the final result will be hardship and may end in bloodshed.'" ([*The Bahá’í Response to Racial Injustice and Pursuit of Racial Unity: Part 1 (1912-1996)*](https://siftersearch.com/Baha'i/14216) - Richard Thomas)

2. **Community and Institutional Efforts**: Shoghi Effendi highlighted the need for the Bahá'í community to actively seek racial unity and harmony. He emphasized that racial unity is the first principle that must be upheld for the community to grow and thrive. This involves not only welcoming diverse individuals but also removing barriers that prevent racial inclusivity within the community:

   > "Racial unity and harmony, perfect cooperation, and freedom from racial prejudice, is the first Principle. How can we hope to keep the following Principles if we do not keep the first?" ([*Shoghi Effendi's Comments on Racial Diversity in the Bahá’í Community*](https://siftersearch.com/Baha'i/12681) - Sadie Oglesby)

3. **Structural Change and Public Discourse**: The Bahá'í teachings advocate for societal reforms and participation in public discourse to dismantle structural racism. This includes addressing prejudices in social systems and promoting policies that ensure equality and justice for all races. The Bahá'í approach involves both individual and collective actions, such as organizing meetings to discuss racial equality and collaborating with broader movements for social justice.

The Bahá'í approach aligns with secular movements in its commitment to justice and equality but differs in its emphasis on spiritual transformation and the creation of a unified global community. The teachings suggest that true and lasting change requires not only reforming external structures but also transforming the inner attitudes and hearts of individuals.
