### Meaning of 'Lesser Peace' and 'Most Great Peace'

<div class="user-turn" id="round-1">

What does 'the Lesser Peace' actually mean, and why does it precede 'the Most Great Peace'? Is the distinction structural or temporal?

</div>

#### Distinction is structural and temporal

<div class="jafar-turn">

The distinction between the "Lesser Peace" and the "Most Great Peace" in Bahá'í teachings is both structural and temporal. Bahá'u'lláh refers to the "Lesser Peace" as a necessary step, urging leaders to "hold ye fast unto this, the Lesser Peace" to improve conditions (*Tablet to Queen Victoria*). Shoghi Effendi clarifies that the "Lesser Peace" involves political and social agreements among nations, while the "Most Great Peace" is a deeper, spiritual unity that only those "fully conscious of the power of His Revelation" can establish (*The Promised Day is Come*). Thus, the "Lesser Peace" lays the groundwork for the "Most Great Peace," which is a more profound and enduring harmony.

</div>

#### Concepts unique to Bahá'í Faith

<div class="jafar-turn">

Actually, the concepts of the "Lesser Peace" and the "Most Great Peace" are unique to the Bahá'í Faith and don't appear explicitly in the Gospels or the Qur'án. In Bahá'í teachings, the "Lesser Peace" involves political and social agreements, as Shoghi Effendi notes, ["hold ye fast unto this, the Lesser Peace"](https://siftersearch.com/document/8302) — a step toward improving conditions. The "Most Great Peace," however, signifies a deeper spiritual unity that transcends political arrangements, achievable only through embracing Bahá'u'lláh's teachings. While the Gospels and the Qur'án emphasize peace and unity, the specific framework of these two stages is distinctively Bahá'í.

</div>

#### Peace as internal and societal concept

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Gospels and the Qur'án indeed discuss peace as both an internal and societal concept, though not in the specific terms used in Bahá'í teachings. In the Gospels, Jesus speaks of peace as a spiritual state, encouraging followers to find inner tranquility. Meanwhile, the Qur'án emphasizes justice and harmony, urging believers to ["enter into submission entirely"](https://siftersearch.com/library/Baha'i/Core%20Tablets/the-bab_suratul-mulk_fa#p1450), which suggests a broader societal peace. These teachings collectively suggest that true peace involves both personal serenity and communal well-being.

#### Texts address personal and societal peace

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Gospels and the Qur'án indeed touch on peace, though not in the specific stages as described in Bahá'í teachings. In the Gospels, the focus is often on personal peace through redemption and morality. The Qur'án, on the other hand, emphasizes societal peace through justice and community harmony. For instance, it calls on rulers to ["heal the dissensions that divide you"](https://siftersearch.com/document/8302) and to act justly, which implies a form of societal peace. While these texts don't outline stages of peace, they certainly address both personal and societal dimensions of it.
