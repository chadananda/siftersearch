Why is the Bahá'í dispensation said to last 1,000 years, not 500 or 5,000? Where does that number come from, and what does it actually mean?

</div>

<div class="jafar-turn">

The Bahá'í dispensation is said to last for about 1,000 years, and this is based on statements by Bahá'u'lláh and 'Abdu'l-Bahá. The figure is not meant to be interpreted with absolute literal precision but rather as a symbolic indication of a long period during which the teachings of Bahá'u'lláh will provide the spiritual and social foundation for humanity's progress.

Bahá'u'lláh, in one of His major works known as the "Kitáb-i-Aqdas," refers to His Revelation as the "source of all learning" that will endure for at least a full millennium. 'Abdu'l-Bahá also reinforced this in His writings, suggesting that the Bahá'í teachings will remain relevant for at least this period.

The concept of a dispensation lasting around 1,000 years reflects a continuity with past religious dispensations, which are often viewed in the context of millennia. It suggests an era where the spiritual principles and social teachings of Bahá'u'lláh are expected to unfold and guide humanity's development until a new Manifestation of God appears. This time frame emphasizes the enduring relevance and transformative potential of Bahá'u'lláh's teachings for the present age.
