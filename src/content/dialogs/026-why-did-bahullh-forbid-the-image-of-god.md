## You

Bahá'u'lláh affirms that God is unknowable in essence and that representations are false. But he also gives many names for God in the Long Obligatory Prayer. How do these fit?

## Jafar

Bahá'u'lláh's teachings present a fascinating interplay between the transcendence and immanence of God. On one hand, Bahá'u'lláh affirms that the essence of God is unknowable and beyond any human comprehension or representation. This is a theme common in many religious traditions, emphasizing the infinite nature of the Divine.

On the other hand, the names and attributes of God, as given in prayers like the Long Obligatory Prayer, serve as a bridge for us to connect with the Divine. These names are not meant to fully encapsulate God's essence — which remains beyond our grasp — but rather to reflect aspects of God's relationship with creation that we can understand and relate to. They are expressions of God's qualities like mercy, justice, and love, which manifest in the world and in our lives.

Thus, while God's essence remains unknowable, these names allow us to draw nearer to God and to strive to embody divine qualities in our own lives. They serve as a means for devotion, reflection, and transformation, helping us orient our hearts and minds toward the divine reality.

## Jafar

You've touched on a profoundly rich aspect of Bahá'í theology, where the unknowability of God's essence coexists with the use of names and attributes, as well as the concept of the Manifestation of God. Let's explore this together.

They are not meant to be comprehensive representations of God's essence but are reflections of divine qualities that we can grasp and strive to emulate. Think of them as windows through which we catch glimpses of the divine light, knowing that the full reality of that light is beyond our view.

The tension you mention arises from our human desire to define and delineate the divine, which by its nature cannot be fully contained within human concepts. Bahá'u'lláh uses these names to guide us toward spiritual growth and understanding, reminding us that while they are tools for connection, they should not be mistaken for the essence itself.

Regarding the Manifestations of God, they play a unique role. They are not God, nor do they encompass God's essence, but they reflect divine attributes in a way that is accessible to humanity. They are both fully human and perfectly reflective of divine qualities, serving as mirrors that reveal God's will and purpose for each age. In this framework, the unknowability of God's essence is not in conflict with the role of the Manifestations. Instead, it highlights the need for these divine educators to bring God's attributes and guidance into the human realm in a form we can understand and follow. The Manifestations are thus a bridge between the infinite and the finite, offering a path for us to know and love God through their teachings and example.
