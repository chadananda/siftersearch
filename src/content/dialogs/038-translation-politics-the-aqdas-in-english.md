## You

The Kitáb-i-Aqdas was written in Arabic in 1873 but only fully translated into English in 1992. Why the delay? What does that delay tell us about how the Bahá'í community handled its own central text?

## Jafar

The delay in translating the Kitáb-i-Aqdas into English until 1992 can be understood in several ways, reflecting both practical considerations and the evolving maturity of the Bahá'í community.

First, the Kitáb-i-Aqdas is a highly complex and multifaceted text, containing laws, ordinances, and exhortations that require careful interpretation and understanding. Translating such a central text demands not only linguistic precision but also a deep understanding of its spiritual and legal implications. The community needed time to develop a mature understanding of its teachings and to establish the administrative structures necessary to implement its laws.

Second, the Bahá'í Faith experienced significant growth in the early 20th century, focusing on spreading the teachings and establishing communities worldwide. The translation of the Kitáb-i-Aqdas was part of a broader process of consolidating these communities and ensuring they were ready to receive and implement the laws of the Aqdas in their lives. Shoghi Effendi, the Guardian of the Bahá'í Faith, and the Universal House of Justice prioritized the translation and dissemination of other works that would lay the foundation for understanding the Aqdas.

The delay also highlights the Bahá'í principle of progressive revelation and the gradual unfolding of religious understanding. The community had to grow in capacity and unity before it could fully embrace the responsibilities and guidance contained in the Kitáb-i-Aqdas. By 1992, the Bahá'í world had reached a level of maturity where the Universal House of Justice felt it was time to make the full text available to English-speaking Bahá'ís and the wider world, marking a significant milestone in the Faith's development.
