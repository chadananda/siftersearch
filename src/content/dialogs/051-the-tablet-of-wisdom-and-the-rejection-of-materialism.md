## You

The Tablet of Wisdom (Lawh-i-Hikmat) addresses the origins of philosophy and rejects pure materialism. How does Bahá'u'lláh distinguish his rejection of materialism from a rejection of science?

## Jafar

While I couldn't locate the specific passage from the Tablet of Wisdom on this topic, Bahá'u'lláh's teachings consistently affirm the harmony of science and religion. His rejection of materialism is not a dismissal of science but a critique of a worldview that sees material reality as the only reality. He distinguishes between the empirical investigation of the physical world, which is the domain of science, and a philosophical materialism that denies spiritual realities and the existence of the soul. In Bahá'í thought, true science and true religion are seen as complementary paths to understanding reality, each necessary for a full comprehension of the world and our place within it. This approach encourages a balance where material progress is guided by spiritual principles.

## Jafar

Here's a passage that reflects the Bahá'í perspective on the harmony between science and religion, distinguishing between materialism and science:

> "Should a man try to fly with the wing of religion alone he would quickly fall into the quagmire of superstition, whilst on the other hand, with the wing of science alone he would also make no progress, but fall into the despairing slough of materialism." ([*The Challenge of Bahá'u'lláh*](https://siftersearch.com/search?query=The%20Challenge%20of%20Bah%C3%A1'u'll%C3%A1h) — Gary L. Matthews)

In this view, Bahá'u'lláh emphasizes that science and religion are both essential for a complete understanding of reality. Science, when devoid of spiritual insight, risks becoming materialistic, focusing solely on the physical and measurable. Conversely, religion, without the grounding of science and reason, can devolve into superstition. Bahá'í teachings prioritize neither over the other; instead, they advocate for a balance where empirical evidence and spiritual truths coexist, each informing and enhancing the understanding of the other. This harmony allows for a fuller exploration of reality, acknowledging both the seen and the unseen.
