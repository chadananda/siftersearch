## DESCRIBE (arc: Báb-appointee → rival)
Mírzá Yaḥyá (c. 1831–1912), son of the vizier Mírzá Buzurg-i-Núrí by a concubine, was the younger half-brother of Bahá'u'lláh, who helped raise and educate him (the Báb in His own hand "commits Mírzá Yaḥyá to the care of Bahá'u'lláh"). While still a youth (~19), he was nominated by the Báb as a figure-head/nominal head of the Bábí community pending "Him Whom God shall make manifest," receiving the titles Ṣubḥ-i-Azal and Ismu'l-Azal. After the Báb's martyrdom (1850) the Bábí cause fell into disorder; Yaḥyá hid in the mountains of Mázindarán, often disguised as a dervish, and was implicated (via accomplices) in the 1852 attempt on Náṣiri'd-Dín Sháh's life, which triggered the great persecution. He joined Bahá'u'lláh's exile in Baghdád, but as Bahá'u'lláh's prestige rose, jealousy hardened into open opposition. In Adrianople (Edirne) the breach became total: Yaḥyá is reported to have attempted to poison Bahá'u'lláh and to have written to the Ottoman governor against Him. With Bahá'u'lláh's open declaration, the community divided — the overwhelming majority became Bahá'ís, a small remnant the Azalís. Yaḥyá was exiled to Famagusta, Cyprus, where he died in 1912, his sect dwindling to historical insignificance. GPB's verdict is severe ("the credulous and cowardly Mírzá Yaḥyá," the "black-hearted scoundrel").
