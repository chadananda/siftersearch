# The Báb's "Curiosity" Works — Verified GENUINE (flag-R correction)

**Status:** KEEP. A prior catalog pass wrongly flagged several of the Báb's works as
"curiosities / likely mis-attributions." The user has confirmed they are GENUINE works of
the Báb, and corpus research below confirms this against **Nader Saiedi, _Gate of the Heart_
(doc 8632)** and **_Logos and Civilization_ (doc 7165)** plus the documents themselves.

**General principle for the record (do NOT dismiss unusual topics):** The Báb's corpus
deliberately spans practical, scientific, and esoteric genres — grammar/morphology, natural
science, alchemy and the elixir, talismans/temples (_haykal_) and numerology, daily
devotional practices, recipes, and replies to specific questions. Saiedi 8632 catalogues this
breadth from the Báb's own autobibliographies (the _Kitábu'l-Fihrist_, the _Khutbiy-i-
Dhikriyyih_/Sermon of Remembrance, and the _Khutba al-Jidda_ — see also corpus doc 15358,
Vahid Brown, "Autobibliography in the Writings of the Báb," _Lights of 'Irfán_ vol. 6). An
unusual topic is therefore a signal to **verify via Saiedi**, never a reason to flag as
mis-attribution. Saiedi is explicit (8632 ¶182) that reading these works as "charms and magic
devices" (MacEoin) is a "literalistic and simplistic" misreading; the Báb's whole symbolic
system treats grammar, number, metal, and talisman as vehicles of metaphysical meaning.

A note on identifiers: the live corpus `docs.metadata` does NOT carry structured Phelps/INBA
fields for these items. Phelps numbers (the _Partial Inventory_, in-corpus doc **8746**,
`BB#####` for the Báb) are not stored per-doc in the DB; INBA numbers appear only embedded in
some OceanLibrary filenames. Where an INBA number is recoverable from the filename it is given;
otherwise the field is marked `not-in-DB` and should be back-filled from Phelps 8746 / OL.

## 1. A Reply to a Question about the Alchemical Elixir — **doc 15099** — GENUINE

- **Authorship:** GENUINE — author "The Báb." Confirmed.
- **What it is:** A short mystical-alchemical treatise (6 paras, English tr.) responding to a
  question about *al-iksír* (the elixir) / *al-ḥajar* (the philosopher's stone). The
  translator's introduction sets the lineage explicitly: both Shaykh Aḥmad al-Aḥsá'í (d. 1826)
  and his successor Sayyid Kāẓim Rashtí (d. 1844) "wrote a good deal about alchemical matters
  and the nature of the so-called 'Philosopher's Stone' (al-ḥajar)," and "both the Báb and
  Bahá'-Allāh have responded to questions about this issue." The Báb's reply uses dense
  alchemical-spiritual imagery: every created thing holds "the letter [trace] of the Elixir"
  (*ḥarf al-iksīr*); the seeker journeys to the divine Throne, kindles the Sinaitic "Fire"
  (the Greatest Name), places "the retort (*al-qar'*) upon the brim of the alembic (*ḥadd
  al-inbīq*)," and produces "the greatest crimson oil (*duhn al-aḥmar al-akbar*) ... more
  rapidly than the twinkling of an eye." The work closes by naming the practice "the Art of
  the Elixir" (*ṣan'a al-iksīr*).
- **Saiedi's treatment / context (doc 8632):** Saiedi devotes sustained analysis to the elixir
  as a core symbol of the Báb's hermeneutics. The elixir "alludes to the symbolism of alchemy,
  which ... was not merely concerned with changing base metal into gold; more significantly,
  the physical process was a symbol of spiritual transformation" (8632 ¶168). He glosses the
  Báb's "spirit of the Elixir of true knowledge" as "the supreme agent that transforms
  potentialities into actualities, the catalyst for the beautification and elevation of being"
  (¶169); "the elixir represents the union of opposites, the unity of the First and the Last"
  (¶364); "the early writings of the Báb refer to His station as the station of the 'true
  Elixir'" (¶365). He ties it directly to the philosopher's-stone tradition: "the elixir (from
  Arabic, *al-iksir*) was also referred to as the 'philosopher's stone' and ... associated with
  the symbol of the 'point' as the 'starting point of creation'" (¶363), quoting the Báb:
  "a true believer is rarer than the philosopher's stone" (¶240, from *Tafsír Súrih Kawthar*,
  INBA 53:181). The verbatim language of doc 15099 ("crimson oil," "twinkling of an eye,"
  Sinaitic Fire) matches Saiedi's documented "crimson Elixir" motif (e.g. 8632 ¶314, ¶783).
  *Logos* corroborates that Bahá'u'lláh too discussed "the techniques of the alchemical
  'elixir' ... only because of the believers' frequent requests" (7165 ¶91) and used the
  "Divine Elixir, which, swift as the twinkling of an eye, transmuteth the souls of men"
  (7165 ¶412–414).
- **Timeline / stage:** Belongs to the Báb's early Shaykhí-inflected esoteric writings (the
  1844–1846 period of replies to questioners). Datable only loosely; the alchemical-reply
  genre is attested across the autobibliographies of A.H. 1260–1262.
- **catalog_ids:** `{ phelps: not-in-DB (look up in 8746 BBxxxxx), inba: not-in-DB,
  oceanlibrary: Tablet Translations/The Bab — "A Reply to a Question about the Alchemical
  Elixir", doc_id: 15099 }`
- **Significance:** A direct, named instance of the Báb writing on alchemy/the elixir — the
  exact genre wrongly flagged. Saiedi treats the elixir as one of the central symbols of the
  Báb's metaphysics, so this is not a curiosity but core to His thought. **KEEP.**

## 2. Recipe for White Tea — **doc 15100** — GENUINE

- **Authorship:** GENUINE — author "The Báb," tr. William McCants.
- **What it is:** A practical/devotional dietary instruction (2 paras). Source cited in the
  doc itself: the Báb's **_Ṣaḥífa bayna'l-Ḥaramayn_** ("Scripture between the Two Sanctuaries"),
  Muḥarram 1261 / mid-January 1845, during His pilgrimage to Mecca and Medina. Directs the
  believer, an hour after sunrise, to take frankincense (*kundur*) and cloves (*qaranful*) with
  pure sugar, and to "drink a cup of water in which the white leaf from China has been boiled,"
  which "refines the (human) constitution, draws out moisture, and closes the paths of
  allusions" — framed devotionally ("benefits ... for those who openly bear witness to the path
  of the bayān") and "God loves singularity under all conditions."
- **Saiedi's treatment / context (doc 8632):** Saiedi confirms and dates the parent work. In
  his catalogue of early works (8632 ¶102) he lists the **_Ṣaḥífiy-i-A'mál-i-Sáních_** (Epistle
  on the Devotional Deeds of the Year), "written between Mecca and Medina, on the first day of
  A.H. 1261, in response to questions posed by Mírzá Muḥíṭ-i-Kirmání and Siyyid 'Alíy-i-Kirmání
  ... Consisting of an introduction and seven chapters, it affirms the station of the Báb,
  answers various questions regarding talismanic symbols, and offers prayers and visitation
  tablets for the rites associated with ... spiritual journey and pilgrimage." Saiedi repeatedly
  cites the **_Ṣaḥífiy-i-Bayna'l-Ḥaramayn_** as a genuine pilgrimage-period work (8632 ¶183–190),
  precisely the manuscript family from which the white-tea passage is drawn — placing a daily
  dietary/devotional rule squarely inside an authenticated, datable text.
- **Timeline / stage:** Pilgrimage to Mecca/Medina, Muḥarram A.H. 1261 = mid-January 1845 —
  the early post-Declaration period.
- **catalog_ids:** `{ phelps: not-in-DB (parent Ṣaḥífa bayna'l-Ḥaramayn = BBxxxxx in 8746),
  inba: not-in-DB, oceanlibrary: Tablet Translations/The Bab — "Recipe for White Tea",
  doc_id: 15100 }`
- **Significance:** Shows the Báb addressing daily practice/diet within revealed scripture —
  the "practical" genre. Genuine, datable, and anchored to a Saiedi-authenticated work. **KEEP.**

## 3. Sun salutation — **doc 16071** — GENUINE

- **Authorship:** GENUINE — by the Báb, with commentary by Bahá'u'lláh; tr. William McCants.
  (Note the DB author field is "The Báb, Bahá'u'lláh," reflecting the joint structure.)
- **What it is:** A devotional practice (8 paras). The core is the Báb's verse from the
  **_Arabic Bayán_ 7:17** (cited in-text): while facing the sun on Friday, recite —
  "Verily, the glory (*al-bahá'*) of God be upon your rising, O rising sun! Testify to that
  which God hath testified of His Own Self ..." Bahá'u'lláh's commentary (excerpted in
  **_Má'idih-yi Ásmání_ 8:104–5**) interprets "the sun" as His own Beauty ("what he meant by
  the sun is my beauty that has shown forth from behind the clouds") and identifies the verse
  as disclosing the Greatest Name — a Friday turning-toward-God ordained "as one of the
  fundamentals of the divine commands revealed in the Bayán."
- **Saiedi's treatment / context (doc 8632):** Sun/fire imagery is one of the Báb's most
  pervasive symbol systems in Saiedi's reading. The Báb's writings are "filled with fire
  imagery" (8632 ¶256); the Point is called "the Eternal Sun" (¶476); the "crystal water"
  whose inner essence is "eternal fire" runs throughout (¶229, ¶236, ¶256). A Friday
  sun-salutation verse fits directly into this attested Bábí solar/fire symbolism — the rising
  sun as a sign (*áya*) testifying to divine unity — and is reinforced by the Báb's own
  statement (in doc 3926) that "the sun is a sign from Me ... in every Manifestation."
- **Timeline / stage:** The _Arabic Bayán_ belongs to the Báb's later Máh-Kú/Chihríq period
  of His major legislative works (c. 1847–1848); the practice it ordains is part of the Bayánic
  ritual system. Bahá'u'lláh's commentary is later (the Edirne/'Akká era of the _Má'idih_
  material).
- **catalog_ids:** `{ phelps: not-in-DB (Arabic Bayán = BBxxxxx in 8746), inba: not-in-DB,
  oceanlibrary: Tablet Translations/Baha'u'llah — "Sun salutation" (Báb + Bahá'u'lláh),
  doc_id: 16071 }`
- **Significance:** A genuine Bayánic devotional practice with an authoritative Bahá'u'lláh
  interpretation; the "sun salutation" label is a translator's gloss, not a sign of
  mis-attribution. **KEEP.**

## 4. Risāla fī al-naḥw wa al-ṣarf / "A Grammar of the Divine" — **doc 57333** — GENUINE

(Plus two closely related grammar/morphology works in the corpus: **doc 5801** and **doc 5819**.)

- **Authorship:** GENUINE — author "The Báb"; tr. William F. McCants ("A Most Noble Pattern:
  Collected Essays on the Writings of the Báb," 2012; bahai-library.com/mccants_grammar_divine).
- **What it is (57333):** The Báb's treatise on Arabic grammar and morphology (*naḥw* =
  syntax, *ṣarf* = morphology). Per the in-corpus description: "The Báb employs classical Arabic
  grammatical categories — noun, verb, particle — as metaphors for levels of divine
  manifestation, transforming an academic discipline into a vehicle for mystical and
  theological instruction ... an early systematic exposition of His theological framework."
  (NB: the corpus currently holds the bibliographic record + description; the full
  ~19,700-word McCants translation is "pending full re-ingestion" — a re-ingest TODO, not an
  authenticity problem.)
- **Related corpus works:**
  - **doc 5801 — _Bayán fí 'Ilm'il-Jawámid wa'l-Mushtaqqát_** (the "Treatise on the Science of
    the Indeclinables and Derivatives" — i.e. the morphology treatise; this is the
    `074-Bayan-fi-Ilm-al-Jawamid-wal-Mushtaqat` file named in the task). Arabic source text in
    corpus (3 paras): the Báb explains *al-jawámid* (fixed/indeclinable forms) and
    *al-mushtaqqát* (derived forms) as cosmological symbols — "God created the point of the
    science of indeclinables and derivatives from the water of this pure ocean."
  - **doc 5819 — "Answer to Ḥájí Mullá Mihdí-i-Rashtí, on grammar."** Arabic source text
    (4 paras). The Báb maps the three Arabic case-endings to divine names: "every agent
    (*fá'il*) is *marfū'* [nominative] for the manifestation of God's name the Withholder
    (*al-Qābiḍ*); every object (*maf'ūl*) is *manṣūb* [accusative] for the name the Quickener
    (*al-Muḥyī*); every genitive (*muḍāf ilayhi*) is *majrūr* for the name the Living
    (*al-Ḥayy*) ... all the rules of grammar (*naḥw*) branch off from these manifestations."
- **Saiedi's treatment / context (doc 8632):** Saiedi treats the grammar works as a genuine and
  characteristic genre. "He approaches the topic of Arabic language and grammar. In one of His
  works, the **Treatise on Grammar (Risálah fi'n-Naḥv)**, He addresses the education of
  children with reference to grammar, reading, and arithmetic" (8632 ¶726). "Arabic grammar is
  to be taught as a symbol of spiritual grammar: children are to learn the words as symbols of
  the Logos ... one of its main elements is the reinterpretation of the three categories of
  the verb, noun, and preposition" (¶727). He describes a second tablet — clearly doc 5819 —
  "in answer to a number of questions including some concerning the rules of Arabic grammar.
  The Báb says that He has not studied the normal science of grammar, and that the questioner
  must concentrate on the spiritual reality of syntax" (¶730), then maps the nominative
  (*marfū'*), accusative (*manṣūb*), and genitive cases onto spiritual stations — exactly the
  *fá'il/maf'ūl* scheme in doc 5819. Saiedi also notes the pedagogical/child-education frame
  (¶732). Crucially, the grammar dispute is historically attested at the Báb's 1848 Tabríz
  examination: the clerics "questioned His authority since He was disregarding the traditional
  rules of grammar" (¶79) — so the Báb's reinterpretation of grammar is woven into His public
  ministry, not a fringe oddity.
- **Timeline / stage:** Early-to-middle period; the child-education/grammar material and the
  replies to questioners date to the 1844–1848 span, and grammar is a live issue at the 1848
  Tabríz trial.
- **catalog_ids:**
  - 57333: `{ phelps: not-in-DB (Risāla fī al-naḥw = BBxxxxx in 8746), inba: not-in-DB,
    oceanlibrary: Tablet Translations/The Bab — "Risala fi al-nahw wa al-sarf (A Grammar of the
    Divine)" tr. McCants, doc_id: 57333 }`
  - 5801: `{ phelps: not-in-DB, inba: not-in-DB, oceanlibrary:
    Core Tablets/The Báb/074-Bayan-fi-Ilm-al-Jawamid-wal-Mushtaqat.md, doc_id: 5801 }`
  - 5819: `{ phelps: not-in-DB, inba: not-in-DB, oceanlibrary:
    Core Tablets/The Báb/009-Answer-to-Haji-Mulla-Mihdi-i-Rashti-on-grammar.md, doc_id: 5819 }`
- **Significance:** A genuine grammar/morphology genre, explicitly catalogued by Saiedi and
  tied to the Báb's pedagogy and to His 1848 examination. **KEEP all three.**

## 5. Other unusual-topic Báb works the catalog under-weighted (scan results)

These surfaced when scanning author "The Báb" for science/alchemy/talisman/practice genres.
All GENUINE; flagged here so they are not dismissed as curiosities.

- **doc 3926 — "Treatise on the Science of Letters and Divine Knowledge" (`110-INBA-086`).**
  45 paras, Arabic source. Heavily numerological/letter-science (*'ilm al-ḥurúf*) and, in its
  later units, **legislative/practical** material in Bayánic style — laws on marriage, burial
  (bodies washed five times, buried in crystal or polished stone, ring inscribed), the
  nineteen-month year, the *mithqál*/*ḥimmaṣ* weights, prayer (19 *rak'ahs* noon-to-noon),
  diet, the calling of the *mu'adhdhin*, even an injunction that a teacher "not strike me before
  five years have passed." This is a substantive work mixing letter-science, numerology, and
  daily practice — exactly the breadth Saiedi documents. **KEEP.**
  `catalog_ids: { phelps: not-in-DB, inba: INBA-086 (from filename), oceanlibrary:
  Core Tablets/The Báb/110-INBA-086.md, doc_id: 3926 }`

- **doc 11752 / 16720 — Excerpts from the _Risáliy-i-Dhahabiyyih_** ("The Golden Treatise"),
  tr. Keven Brown (2001). Philosophical/metaphysical treatise: the Báb critiques the
  Illuminationist, Peripatetic, Ṣadrian, and theosopher schools, and refutes *waḥdat al-wujúd*
  (unity of existence), distinguishing the divine Essence from the creative Will (*mashiyyat*).
  The title echoes the "golden treatise" (*al-risála al-dhahabiyya*) medical/alchemical genre
  attributed to Imám Riḍá; Saiedi (8632 ¶75) carefully untangles the naming confusion among
  *Khutbiy-i-Dhikriyyih*, *Risála-yi Dhahabiyya*, and *Ṣaḥífiy-i-Raḍawiyyih* (correcting
  Nicolas/MacEoin), confirming "Dhahabiyyih" as a distinct genuine work. **KEEP.**
  `catalog_ids: { phelps: not-in-DB, inba: not-in-DB, oceanlibrary: Tablet Translations/The
  Bab — "Excerpts from the Risáliy-i-Dhahabiyyih", doc_id: 11752 / 16720 }`

- **Talisman / temple (_haykal_) / circle works — genre confirmation (no single doc flagged,
  but the genre must not be flagged).** Saiedi 8632 ¶182–191, ¶1167–1172 treats the **Tablet of
  Nineteen Temples** (_Lawḥ-i-Hurúfát_ / Tablet of the Letters), the pentagram **_haykal_** for
  men and the **circle** (*dá'ira*) for women (Persian Bayán 5:gate 10), and short works
  "written in this pentagram form." He explicitly rejects MacEoin's "talisman/magic device"
  reading: these are "symbolic designs which express spiritual meanings," not magic charms
  (¶191, ¶1172). The numerology (*abjad*: *báb*=5, *Huva*=11, names of the Báb=202=*rabb*,
  etc.) is likewise a genuine, central feature (¶342, ¶368–369). Any corpus doc carrying
  *haykal*/temple/circle/letter-science content is therefore GENUINE genre, **KEEP** — verify
  via Saiedi, never flag.

## Note on the breadth of the Báb's corpus (for the record)

The Báb's writings are vast and generically diverse by design. Beyond the well-known doctrinal
and legislative books (Qayyúmu'l-Asmá', the Persian and Arabic Bayán, the Dalá'il-i-Sab'ih /
Seven Proofs, the Kitáb-i-Panj-Sha'n), His corpus deliberately includes **practical/scientific
and esoteric genres**: alchemy and the elixir (doc 15099; the philosopher's-stone tradition of
Shaykh Aḥmad and Sayyid Kāẓim), natural science and cosmology (Risáliy-i-Dhahabiyyih 11752/16720;
Kitábu'r-Ruh on natural phenomena, Saiedi 8632 ¶99), grammar and morphology (57333, 5801, 5819),
letter-science and numerology (3926; *abjad* throughout), talismans/temples/circles (the
Nineteen Temples, the pentagram *haykal*, the women's circle), and daily devotional and dietary
practice (the white-tea recipe 15100 in the Ṣaḥífa bayna'l-Ḥaramayn; the Friday sun-salutation
16071). Saiedi's _Gate of the Heart_ (8632) and _Logos and Civilization_ (7165) document this
breadth and repeatedly warn against the literalist/"magic charm" misreading that produced the
original flag-R. **Operating rule: when an unusual-topic work appears under "The Báb," treat it
as a genuine work-entity and verify via Saiedi (8632/7165) and the document itself — do not
flag it as a mis-attribution.**
