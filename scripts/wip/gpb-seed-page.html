Building Entities from Seed

To find every passage about a person across thousands of books, a search engine first has to know who's who. SifterSearch learns that not from the whole library at once, but from a single trusted book first — a “seed” — and grows outward. This page explains, for technical and non-technical readers alike, what a seed is and why it matters, then what that first book (Shoghi Effendi's God Passes By) is and why it makes such a good starting point for Bahá'í history.

What a seed is, and why it matters

Underneath the library, SifterSearch keeps a structured who's-who: one record per real person, place, or written work, gathering every name that thing is known by. This is harder than it sounds. The same figure appears under wildly different names — a birth name, an honorific, a conferred title, several spellings, sometimes a different alphabet — while different people share the same common name. Without a who's-who, a search for one person misses most of the passages about them and confuses them with everyone else who shares a name.

You cannot build that reliably by turning a program loose on ten thousand books at once. Picture a new archivist handed a thousand overlapping memoirs of the same historical movement, in several languages, with no roster to start from. They will inevitably file one person under a dozen headings and merge three different people who happen to share a name. That tangle is far harder to undo later than it would have been to avoid.

A seed is the fix. It is a small, dense, carefully verified core — the central figures, places, works, and events, each with its correct name, all its aliases, and clear notes on how to tell look-alikes apart — built first. Every book processed afterward is matched against that trusted core instead of inventing new records from scratch. The index grows by accumulation; it does not fragment.

Get the seed right and every later book inherits a clean foundation. Get it wrong and every later book inherits the mistake. That asymmetry is the whole reason the seed is kept small and checked by hand — and it is why the choice of which book to seed from matters so much.

Introducing God Passes By

The seed book is God Passes By: a history of the first hundred years (1844–1944) of the Bábí and Bahá'í religions, written in 1944 by Shoghi Effendi. For readers new to this: Shoghi Effendi (1897–1957) led the Bahá'í Faith from 1921 until his death. He was the great-grandson of its founder, Bahá'u'lláh, and bore the title Guardian of the Faith.

One feature of his role is what makes this book decisive. Within the Bahá'í Faith, Shoghi Effendi held a uniquely defined office: he was its authorized interpreter — the one person empowered to state authoritatively what the religion's scriptures and history mean, and how its figures and events are to be ranked. So when God Passes By describes someone as central or peripheral, as a hero or a betrayer, that judgment is not one historian's opinion among many; within the Faith it is the settled, authoritative reading.

Why it works as the seed nucleus

For seeding the who's-who of Bahá'í history, God Passes By is the right choice on plain technical grounds and on doctrinal grounds — and the two reinforce each other.

The technical case

  • Clean, consistent spelling. Transliteration of Persian and Arabic names varies enormously from book to book. God Passes By uses one careful, consistent system, giving a reliable canonical spelling for each name onto which every other variant can be mapped.
  • High signal, low noise. The prose is spare and every name is deliberate, so the seed fills with figures that genuinely matter rather than incidental mentions.
  • Comprehensive but small. A single, hand-checkable volume spans the entire first century, so one book yields the backbone of the whole index.

The doctrinal case

Because its author was the Faith's authorized interpreter, God Passes By is the authoritative source for how a person, place, work, or event is to be understood and weighed. When two sources disagree about a figure's significance or role, this book settles it. Founding the index on it means the meaning attached to each entity rests on the most authoritative reading available — not on a guess, and not on whichever account happened to be read first.

Two axes: facts versus meaning

It helps to split the questions the index answers, because different sources are trusted for each.

AxisThe questionWho is trusted
IdentificationWho is this person? What are the dates? Which work is meant?Primary documents and careful historians — reliable for facts.
CharacterizationHow significant is this figure? What is their role? How is an act to be framed?God Passes By is primary — above later scholarship, and above more detailed histories.

The characterization rule has one subtlety worth stating plainly, because it is easy to misread. It does not claim that an interpreter outranks scripture — in the Bahá'í Faith the revealed Word is supreme as revelation. The claim is narrower: the task of interpreting — explaining what the Faith's elements mean and how they rank — was Shoghi Effendi's authorized role, so for that interpretive purpose his reading governs, even over an individual's own reading of a sacred text. Two consequences follow:

  • A later authorized interpretation supersedes an earlier one. Where readings differ over time, the most recent authoritative one stands.
  • Translation is itself interpretation. An authorized translator's word choice fixes which sense an ambiguous term carries. The word “Bayán,” for example, can mean a specific book by the Báb, another distinct book, His whole Revelation, the community of His followers, or simply “utterance” — and the chosen translation, not a guess, decides which is meant.

The foundation and the tower

Picture the whole collection as a skyscraper, built from the ground down before it can rise. Each layer is laid only after the one beneath it is solid, and the deeper a layer sits, the more weight it carries — so the more it is worth verifying by hand.

  • God Passes By — the footer. The deepest footing: small, but it bears everything above it. Extracted in full, then every significant figure confirmed by hand — correct name, every alias and spelling, the book's exact wording, family and allegiance, and explicit notes that keep look-alikes apart.
  • The Dawn-Breakers — the slab. Nabíl's detailed narrative of the same early period, poured onto the footer. Together these two books are the foundation — a small share of all the names, but the key ones nearly every later book collides with — so Dawn-Breakers earns the same hand-verification as God Passes By, not a lighter pass.
  • Taherzadeh, Balyuzi, and Mázandarání — the first floor. The major detailed histories (including works in Arabic and Persian script), resolved against the foundation rather than re-derived from scratch.
  • Momen and the contemporary-account scholarship — the next several stories up from the first floor.
  • The remaining thousands of documents — the tower. Each rises on the dense, verified structure already beneath it, resolving against it instead of fragmenting into duplicates.

Every level is laid only after the ones below it are solid. The index is cumulative: each book both draws on the structure beneath and adds to it. The hardest, most variable sources become manageable precisely because the footer and the slab were laid, by hand, first.

Why it must be verified by hand

A seed is leverage, and leverage cuts both ways. One wrong merge — folding two distinct people into a single record — or one wrong split — scattering one person across several — does not stay contained; it spreads into every book that resolves against the seed. So the standard for this stage is high:

  • Look-alikes are pinned down explicitly. The cast is full of collisions — several men share the name Mullá Ḥusayn; three different officials are called Ḥusayn Khán; the title “Gate of the Gate” belongs to one disciple, not to the Báb. Each is recorded as a rule that later books carry forward.
  • Claims are quoted, not paraphrased. A characterization is recorded in the book's own words, with its location, so a description is never silently invented or pinned on the wrong source.
  • Absence is checked, not assumed. Because spelling varies so widely, a careless search misses text that is really there. A passage is hunted thoroughly before it is called missing — a false “not found” is as damaging as a fabrication.
  • Nuance is kept. The book is even-handed even toward its antagonists; the record preserves that nuance instead of flattening a figure into a label.

Status

The footer is in: God Passes By has been extracted and verified — the central figures, the early precursors and disciples, and the figures, works, and events of the later ministry, each with its aliases and its look-alike notes. The Dawn-Breakers — the slab — is being processed against it now, completing the foundation. The tower rises next.
